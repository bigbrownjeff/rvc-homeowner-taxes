## 1. §467 senior exemption income tiers — VERDICT: WRONG (claim shifts all tiers up ~$8,400; your correction is right, plus the top tier is now 65%)

**Actual Nassau County tier table (county + town + school taxes), per official Nassau Dept. of Assessment brochure (Rev. 3-26, for 2027-28 filings):**

| 2025 income | Exemption |
|---|---|
| ≤ $47,000 | **65%** |
| $47,001–$47,999 | 60% |
| $48,000–$48,999 | 55% |
| $49,000–$50,000 | **50%** |
| $50,001–$50,999 | 45% |
| $51,000–$51,999 | 40% |
| $52,000–$52,999 | 35% |
| $53,000–$53,899 | 30% |
| $53,900–$54,799 | 25% |
| $54,800–$55,699 | 20% |
| $55,700–$56,599 | 15% |
| $56,600–$57,499 | 10% |
| $57,500–$58,399 | **5%** |

- **$58,399 is the ceiling for ANY exemption (the 5% floor tier)** — not the threshold for the full 50%. The 50% tier requires income ≤ $50,000 (statutory base maximum, RPTL §467(3)(a)).
- **There is no $66,799 anywhere.** The claim apparently took the real table and shifted every tier up by $8,400. No NY sliding-scale option extends past $58,399 under the $50,000 base (DTF: 5% tier max = $58,400 exclusive).
- Nuance the claim also misses: since the December 2025 law (see #8), Nassau's scale now runs **5%–65%**, not 5–50%. Town of Hempstead's published table (vintage: 2024 tax year) still shows a 50% top tier ≤ $50,000 → 5% at $57,500–$58,399. Village of RVC publishes only the $58,399 ceiling; its internal tier table (50% vs 65% top) was not published online.
- Sources: Nassau brochure — https://www.nassaucountyny.gov/DocumentCenter/View/49404/ (Rev. 3-26, March 2026); DTF senior exemption page — https://www.tax.ny.gov/pit/property/exemption/seniorexempt.htm (current, reflects 2025 law); Town of Hempstead — https://hempsteadny.gov/395/Senior-Citizen (2024 vintage).

## 6. STAR exemption reimbursed by state; post-2015 buyers get credit — VERDICT: CONFIRMED

- Statute: **RPTL §1306-a** — "The total tax savings duly provided by each school district pursuant to this section shall be a state charge," paid as state aid on comptroller's audit/warrant after DTF certification. STAR is the one exemption that does **not** shift burden or cut district revenue. https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/laws/RPT/1306-A
- DTF: STAR exemption is "closed to all new applicants"; "If you're a new homeowner or you weren't receiving the STAR exemption on your current home in 2015, you can register for the STAR credit" (check/direct deposit from NYS). https://www.tax.ny.gov/pit/property/star/star-exemption-program.htm
- Corroborating: NYC IBO STAR paper ("the state reimburses the districts for the difference... essentially another source of state aid").

## 7. Levy mechanics: §467 shifts burden, doesn't cut district revenue — VERDICT: CONFIRMED

- NY school districts levy a fixed dollar amount (the levy), apportioned over **taxable** assessed value; exemptions reduce the tax base, raising the rate on everyone else.
- DTF (via OSC search corpus): exemptions "are paid for by increases in property taxes on all other taxpayers (except for the STAR exemption, which is funded directly by New York State)."
- OSC audit guidance: "Every exemption granted on a property shifts the tax burden to the non-exempt properties." (OSC, Town of Plattsburgh exemption audit — https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/audits/pdf/plattsburgh.pdf; see also OSC research brief "Property Tax Exemptions in New York State" — https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/publications/pdf/propertytax_exemptions_0.pdf)
- DTF state-land report: "the more tax-exempt property there is in a jurisdiction, the greater the tax rate generally is on the owners of taxable property." https://www.tax.ny.gov/research/property/reports/landcomp/section2.htm

## 8. S5175A (2025) — VERDICT: CONFIRMED as to content, but status OUTDATED in claim: it is now LAW

- S5175A (Comrie) authorizes localities to grant **up to 65%** §467 exemption via tiers below the locality's maximum income level M: ≥M−$1,000 → 50%; M−$1,000 to M−$2,000 → 55%; M−$2,000 to M−$3,000 → 60%; < M−$3,000 → 65%.
- **Passed Senate June 11, 2025; signed December 5, 2025 — Chapter 581 of the Laws of 2025**; applies to taxable years beginning Jan 1, 2026. Now codified at RPTL §467(1)(b). Nassau's 2026 brochure already reflects it (65% ≤ $47,000, i.e., M=$50,000).
- Sources: https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/bills/2025/S5175/amendment/A; Governor's approval release: https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-hochul-authorizes-real-property-tax-exemptions-new-york-seniors; statute: https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/laws/RPT/467

## 9. Nassau assessment: 0.1% LOA, frozen roll, no sale-triggered reassessment — VERDICT: CONFIRMED (freeze ongoing into 2026-27)

- **Class 1 LOA = 0.1%** since the 2020-21 county-wide reassessment ($350 AV ≈ $350,000 FMV). Confirmed for the 2026/27 roll ("the Assessor has published residential properties at a level of assessment of .1%").
- **Freeze**: county froze the roll after 2020-21 reassessment (freeze began with 2021-22 roll under Curran, continued under Blakeman). The **2026-27 tentative roll published Jan 2, 2026 remains frozen — sixth consecutive frozen roll**; county-wide reassessment has NOT resumed as of June 2026. Grievance window Jan 2–Mar 31, 2026 (extended). Sources: Newsday/LI Herald coverage (Feb 2024, "frozen for a fourth straight year"); Nassau Assessment Review Calendar https://www.nassaucountyny.gov/2321/Assessment-Review-Calendar; assessment-roll page https://www.nassaucountyny.gov/5120/Assessment-Rolls.
- **Sale does NOT trigger reassessment** — CONFIRMED. NY law has no acquisition-value (Prop 13-style) assessment; RPTL §305(2) requires all parcels in an assessing unit be assessed at a uniform percentage of market value, so a purchase price cannot lawfully reset one parcel's AV. Physical changes (new construction/renovation permits) can change AV even during the freeze; a deed transfer alone does not.

## 10. Transfer taxes — VERDICT: CONFIRMED, with useful history on the Nassau question

- **NYS real estate transfer tax: $2 per $500 (0.4%)**, payable by the **grantor (seller)**; it is a NY State tax (Tax Law Art. 31) — no part goes to county/village/school district. https://www.tax.ny.gov/bus/transfer/rptidx.htm
- **Mansion tax: 1% of consideration on residential conveyances ≥ $1,000,000, paid by the grantee (buyer)** — statewide; the graduated extra rates (up to 3.9% total) apply **only in NYC** ($2M+). Same DTF source.
- **Nassau county-level transfer tax: none currently.** Tax Law **Article 31-E** (1999) authorized Nassau to impose $5 per $500 (1%), but the authority **expired January 31, 2001** (Tax Law §1449-bbb) and was not renewed. Websites quoting a "Nassau County transfer tax" of $4/$1,000 are just restating the state 0.4% tax. (Nassau does charge recording and tax-map verification *fees*, which are not transfer taxes.) https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/laws/TAX/1449-BBB

## 11. 2% levy cap and tax base growth factor — VERDICT: CONFIRMED (and yes, physical renovations raise the allowable levy)

- Formula (General Municipal Law §3-c for local govts; Education Law §2023-a for school districts): prior-year levy × **tax base growth factor** (only if >1) + prior PILOTs − prior capital/tort exclusions, × **allowable levy growth factor = lesser of 1.02 or (1 + inflation factor)** (floor 1.0), − coming-year PILOTs + carryover; plus exclusions (pension contribution rate growth > 2 percentage points; tort judgments > 5% of prior levy; school capital levy). School district override requires 60% voter approval.
- **Tax base growth factor** = DTF-certified factor reflecting "physical or quantity change" in taxable full value (GML §3-c referencing RPTL §1220 determination). OSC glossary: quantity change = "increase in assessed value... resulting from new construction, property annexed..., and the addition of property omitted from the prior roll" — i.e., **construction and capital improvements, never market appreciation**, and it is never negative. https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/property-tax-cap/real-property-tax-cap-information-glossary; https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/laws/GMU/3-C; DTF TBGF tables: https://www.tax.ny.gov/research/property/cap.htm
- **So: renovations/additions by new buyers that add assessed value DO flow into the quantity change factor and raise the allowable levy** (the district may levy more without an override). Mere resale or price appreciation does not.

## 12. §467 renewal, income definition, 2022 income-limit law — VERDICT: CONFIRMED with subsection correction

- **Annual renewal**: required by **RPTL §467(6)** (Form RP-467-Rnw). The affidavit-in-lieu option after **5 consecutive years** is **§467(6)(b)** (not §467(7)); Nassau adopted it via county Local Law 13-2020 (stated in the county brochure). https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/laws/RPT/467
- **Income definition**: since the 2024 rolls (Part K, **Chapter 59 of the Laws of 2023**), income = **FAGI** with adjustments — taxable IRA distributions deductible (local opt-out), Social Security not in FAGI added (local option), tax-exempt interest/dividends added, unreimbursed medical/Rx deductible where locally allowed, losses capped $3,000/category, $15,000 total. https://www.tax.ny.gov/pit/property/exemption/459c-467changes.htm
- **2022 law**: **Chapter 488 of the Laws of 2022 (S.3085-A), signed Aug 8, 2022** — raised the maximum allowable base income limit from $29,000 to **$50,000** (sliding scale therefore to $58,399), for §467 and §459-c, first applicable to 2023 rolls. Claim's "Ch. 488 of 2022" is exactly right. https://www.tax.ny.gov/pdf/publications/orpts/legis/legsum22.pdf
- **New (post-dates the claim): the current statute text raises the maximum allowable income cap to $75,000 effective July 1, 2027** (§467(3)(a) as amended by 2025 legislation; see DTF Summary of 2025 RPT Legislation, https://www.tax.ny.gov/pdf/publications/orpts/legis/legsum25.pdf). Localities would still have to opt in.

## 13. California Prop 19 — VERDICT: CONFIRMED (mechanics); fiscal/usage data limited

- Prop 19 (Nov 2020, effective Apr 1, 2021): homeowners **55+** (or disabled, or disaster victims) may transfer the factored base year value of a primary residence to a replacement primary residence **anywhere in the state, of any value** (upward adjustment for the amount above 100-110% of the sold home's value), **up to 3 times**, transactions within 2 years. CA BOE: https://www.boe.ca.gov/prop19/ and Pub. 801: https://www.boe.ca.gov/pdf/pub801.pdf; LA County Assessor: https://assessor.lacounty.gov/homeowners/proposition-19
- **Fiscal results**: LAO's official estimate: base-transfer expansion loses revenue but the paired inheritance-exclusion narrowing more than offsets it — "Local governments could gain tens of millions of dollars of property tax revenue per year... could grow over time to a few hundred million dollars per year" (same for schools). https://lao.ca.gov/BallotAnalysis/Proposition?number=19&year=2020. No consolidated statewide BOE usage count located; county assessor annual reports (LA, Santa Clara) track claim volumes — UNVERIFIED beyond LAO estimate; cite LAO only.

## 14. NY portability / circuit breaker inventory (as of 2026) — VERDICT: CONFIRMED — NY has NO base portability; circuit breakers are small or expired

- **No assessed-value portability**: NY assesses at uniform percentage of market value (RPTL §305); there is no acquisition-value basis, hence nothing to "port." No Prop-19 analog exists.
- **Eligibility continuity quirk**: §467's 12-month ownership requirement is waived if the senior had the exemption on a previously owned property (Nassau brochure: "Unless applicant had a senior exemption on previous Nassau property") — portability of *eligibility timing*, not of tax base.
- **IT-214 Real Property Tax Credit (the circuit breaker, Tax Law §606(e))**: still exists but threadbare — household gross income ≤ **$18,000**, property value ≤ $85,000, credit max **$75 (under 65) / $375 (65+)**; refundable. Unchanged for decades. https://www.tax.ny.gov/pit/credits/real_property_tax_credit.htm
- **IT-229 Real Property Tax Relief Credit** (homeowner credit, income ≤ $250,000, taxes > 6% of income): applied to tax years 2021-2023 only — **discontinued for 2024+**. https://www.tax.ny.gov/pit/credits/real-property-tax-relief-credit.htm
- **Property Tax Relief Credit checks (2016-2019)** ("P-TRC"): expired after 2019.
- Otherwise: STAR/Enhanced STAR (see #3-6), §467/459-c exemptions, NYC SCRIE/DRIE for renters. **Bottom line: a senior who sells in RVC and buys elsewhere in NY carries no tax benefit except re-applying for STAR/§467 at the new home.**

## 15. Senior property-tax deferral comparables — VERDICT: CONFIRMED; three usable examples

1. **Oregon — Senior and Disabled Property Tax Deferral, ORS 311.666–311.701**: state DOR pays the county tax each Nov 15, records a lien, charges **6% interest**; repaid when owner sells, dies, or moves; recertification every 2 years; income cap ~$70,000 (2026, indexed). https://www.oregon.gov/dor/programs/property/pages/senior-and-disabled-property-tax-deferral-program.aspx
2. **Massachusetts — G.L. c. 59, §5, Clause 41A**: seniors 65+ may defer all or part of property taxes until sale or death; local option income limit (default low, may be raised to the state circuit-breaker limit); deferral + interest capped at 50% of fair cash value; statutory interest 8% (communities may lower; 16% post-death until paid). Mass. DLS "Taxpayer's Guide to Local Property Tax Deferrals": https://www.mass.gov/doc/guide-to-real-estate-tax-deferrals-for-qualifying-persons/download
3. **Washington — RCW ch. 84.38**: deferral for 61+/disabled homeowners under an income threshold (county-median-indexed); state pays, lien attaches, **5% interest**; repayable at sale/death; deferral capped at 80% of equity. https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=84.38 and WA DOR deferral program pages.
(Also exists: Texas Tax Code §33.06 — 65+/disabled deferral, 5% interest.)

## Most important corrections

1. **Claim 1 is wrong in the way you suspected, and then some**: $58,399 is the income ceiling for the *minimum* 5% exemption; the full-50% threshold is $50,000; there is no $66,799 tier — and Nassau's county/town/school scale now tops out at **65% for incomes ≤ $47,000** (post-Ch. 581 of 2025), so "5-50%" is itself outdated. Any savings model keyed to "50% at $58,399" materially overstates the benefit for incomes $50k-$58k and understates it below $47k.
2. **Claim 5: use Basic $1,068 / Enhanced $2,856** (DTF 2025 final, RVC Class 1 exemption savings). The $1,220/$2,680 figures match no DTF row. Credit-check recipients: $1,089 / $3,147.01.
3. **Claim 8 status flip: S5175A is no longer a pending bill — it was signed Dec 5, 2025 (Ch. 581 of 2025)**, effective 2026, and Nassau already applies the 65% tier. Related: the §467 maximum allowable income cap rises to **$75,000 effective July 1, 2027** per the amended statute — flag for forward-looking projections.
4. **No Nassau county transfer tax exists today** — the 1999 authorization (Tax Law Art. 31-E, $5/$500) sunset on Jan 31, 2001. Sellers pay only the NYS 0.4% ($2/$500); buyers pay the 1% mansion tax ≥ $1M. None of it funds the county, village, or school district.
5. **RVC UFSD's own §467 adoption is supported only by county-wide statements ("all 62 school districts enacted")** — no district-specific resolution or current district income ceiling was locatable online. Since school taxes are ~60%+ of an RVC bill, confirm the district's adopted tier table (and whether it opted into the 65% option and the public-school-child exception) with the district clerk before publishing. Renewal mechanics correction: the affidavit option is §467(6)(b), not §467(7); Nassau adopted it by Local Law 13-2020.
