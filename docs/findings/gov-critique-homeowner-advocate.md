# Homeowner-Advocate Critique of the Lead Strategist Ranking

**Lens:** Pure dollars back in a *named individual's* pocket, within a realistic horizon (this grievance cycle to ~5 yrs). Every county-abstract, "aggregate savings," or desegregation-payload item is stripped. A lever scores only if it lands on *this* homeowner's bill. The two archetypes:

- **RVC homeowner**: ~$15K total bill, school ~62%. In the **Village of Rockville Centre** — which means the village provides PD, sanitation, water, parks, lighting, *and a municipal electric utility*. **Pays NO town special-district layer.**
- **Unincorporated Town-of-Hempstead homeowner**: pays the special-district stack (sanitation/water/park/lighting/fire) that villages self-provide.

---

## KILL SHOT

**The lead ranks "Commissioner-run special-district takeover (sanitation)" as the #1 homeowner-dollar lever and even calls it "the clean pure-dollar win." For the named RVC homeowner that lever is worth exactly $0.** The report's own redundancy table (GOVERNANCE_REVIEW.md line 42-45) shows the RVC line for town special districts is "✘ (village provides)." An RVC homeowner does not pay a town sanitary-district tax, a town water-district tax, or a town park/lighting-district tax — the village bills them for bundled service instead. So the entire $18M-Hempstead / 3x-cost-gap apparatus the lead leads with **cannot touch an RVC bill**. The lead even names "the RVC/wealthy-side homeowner" throughout and *still* puts a lever with zero RVC incidence at the top of the dollar ranking. It is a Town-of-Hempstead-only lever mislabeled as the universal pure-dollar winner.

Worse, the lead's whole memo never mentions the levers that actually put four-figure dollars on a single RVC bill — **STAR/Enhanced STAR and the §467 senior exemption** — because those aren't "governance" reforms. But the prompt asked for *dollars back in pocket*, not governance elegance. A lever that is already law, already state-funded, and filable this cycle for $2,856/yr beats a referendum-gated patronage fight that yields an RVC homeowner nothing.

## OVERRATED LEVERS (by homeowner-dollar incidence)

1. **Commissioner-run special-district takeover (lead #1).** $0 to RVC (village self-provides). Real but mid-hundreds to a *ToH* homeowner, and only after a referendum beats entrenched commissioners + unions, with wage-harmonization clawing back part of the 3x gap. Correct for the ToH archetype as a top-3, but it is not "the" pure-dollar win and is a non-event for the homeowner the prompt actually named.

2. **Cross-line integration merger (lead #4).** The lead concedes this is "WEAK and likely NEGATIVE" for the RVC homeowner — the wealthy side's rate *rises* to absorb the poorer side, and Nassau districts at ~3,700 pupils are off the steep cost curve. For a pure-dollar lens this isn't merely overrated, it is **negative-dollar** and should be killed outright, not ranked. (Its entire value is the desegregation payload the prompt told us to strip.)

3. **Commercial tax-base pooling (lead #5).** The lead itself says it is "redistributive (revenue-neutral region-wide) rather than cost-cutting" and "for the wealthy side a modest cost." So for the RVC homeowner it is **net-negative or zero dollars**; for a commercial-poor ToH district it is mild relief. Equity instrument, not a dollar lever — strip it.

4. **"County guarantee" / assessment-refund reform (lead #2).** The lead correctly flags it as "mostly DIFFUSE county-budget, not a homeowner line item." County levy is ~14% of the bill; shaving $89M off certiorari is real but reaches an individual bill as pennies-to-low-dollars of county-levy restraint, over a multi-year charter fight. The lead ranks it #2 on homeowner dollars; on *direct* incidence it belongs far below the exemptions it never mentions. Note the lead buries the one genuinely direct effect — *an individual who grieves a real over-assessment gets a refund* — which is actually the underrated lever (see below), not the structural reform.

5. **BOCES back-office consolidation (lead #3 / co-top pick).** The lead's own arithmetic is "$80-160M/yr = 1-2% of $8B aggregate school spend," then concedes savings "routinely overstated" (Michigan food-service fully offset; OSC couldn't verify EPC guarantees). Translate 1-2% of the school line to an RVC bill: school is ~$9,300 of a $15K bill, so a *fully-passed-through* 1-2% is ~$95-185/yr — and it won't fully pass through, it flows to levy restraint diluted across the base. Real, low-risk, but two-figures-to-low-three-figures per home, not the headline win the lead frames. Overranked relative to STAR/467/grievance.

## UNDERRATED LEVERS (the ones the lead omits entirely — these are the dollar winners)

These are not in the lead's list at all because they aren't "governance reform." For a homeowner-dollar lens they are #1-#3.

1. **Enhanced STAR / Basic STAR (the single biggest direct check).** DTF 2025 final, RVC Tax Class 1: **Basic STAR = $1,068/yr**, **Enhanced STAR = $2,856/yr** (exemptions.md lines 53-55). Enhanced eligibility is income ≤ $110,750 (2026) for 65+. This is *state-funded* (RPTL §1306-a — "a state charge"), so unlike §467 it doesn't shift burden to neighbors; it's pure subtraction from the school line. Any RVC senior household not already enrolled in Enhanced STAR is leaving **$2,856/yr** on the table. Filable now. Nothing in the lead's memo comes within an order of magnitude per qualifying home.

2. **§467 senior exemption (stacks where eligible).** Up to **65%** off county+town+school taxes post-Ch. 581 of 2025 for incomes ≤ $47,000, sliding to 5% at $58,399 (exemptions.md lines 8-28). For an income-qualified RVC senior this is a *majority* reduction of the ~$9,300 school portion plus county+town — easily **$3,000-6,000/yr**, and it stacks with Enhanced STAR. Income-limited, so narrower than STAR, but for the households it hits it is the largest single lever on this entire list. (Caveat in exemptions.md line 131: confirm RVC UFSD's adopted ceiling/65% opt-in with the district clerk.)

3. **Grieving the frozen assessment (direct AV cut, every homeowner).** Nassau is on its **sixth consecutive frozen roll**; reassessment has not resumed as of 2026 (exemptions.md line 80). A frozen, stale roll means individual over-assessments harden in place — and a successful grievance cuts *your* AV, hence *your* share of every levy (school + county + town + village), directly and permanently until the freeze lifts. This is the homeowner analogue the lead buried inside its diffuse "county guarantee" lever. The post-2018 protest wave (200K+ protests, ~80K SCAR) exists precisely because this works. No referendum, no Albany, filable in the Jan-Mar window. Typical SCAR wins run hundreds to low-thousands off the annual bill depending on the over-assessment.

**Honorable mention (ToH archetype only):** the special-district takeover the lead overranks for everyone is genuinely a top-3 *for the unincorporated-ToH homeowner* — mid-hundreds/yr where they sit in a high-cost commissioner sanitary district. It just doesn't exist for RVC.

## EQUITY CRITIQUE

The prompt explicitly strips equity/desegregation, and that is the right call for a "my-bill" lens — but it also exposes that the lead's memo is *organized around* equity and only secondarily around dollars. Three of the lead's eight levers (cross-line merger #4, tax-base pooling #5, and most of the desegregation framing) deliver **zero or negative** dollars to the named RVC homeowner; their value is entirely the racism payload. The lead is honest about this ("do NOT promise an RVC homeowner that erasing racism also cuts their bill") — but having conceded it, the lead *still* lets the equity framing crowd the genuinely large dollar levers (STAR/467/grievance) entirely out of the document. The equity-neutral truth: the levers that most cut an RVC bill (exemptions, grievance) are segregation-neutral, and the levers that most reduce segregation (merger, pooling) *raise* an RVC bill. For this homeowner the two axes don't just diverge — they point opposite. A "my-bill" advocate strips the equity levers without apology and is not interested in the "5 Rs."

One real-incidence note the lead misses: a *fairer* assessment roll (the grievance/reassessment machinery) has a documented regressive-correction angle — stale rolls tend to over-assess lower-value parcels — so the grievance lever does a sliver of equity work *as a byproduct* of putting dollars in the homeowner's pocket. That's the only place the two axes touch, and it favors the dollar lever, not the merger.

## MY TOP 3 FOR THE NAMED RVC HOMEOWNER (in order, with numbers)

1. **Enroll in Enhanced STAR if 65+ and income ≤ $110,750 — $2,856/yr** (or Basic STAR $1,068/yr if not yet 65). State-funded, doesn't hit neighbors, filable now. The single largest direct-dollar lever, full stop.
2. **Claim the §467 senior exemption if income-qualified — up to ~65% of county+town+school, i.e. $3,000-6,000/yr, stacks on STAR.** Narrower (income ≤ $58,399) but the biggest number on the page for those it reaches.
3. **Grieve the frozen assessment — typically hundreds to low-thousands/yr off every levy line, permanent until the freeze lifts.** Universal (no income test), filable Jan-Mar, the homeowner version of the "assessment reform" the lead diffused into the county budget.

*If the household is not a senior and STAR/467 don't apply,* the order collapses to: (1) grieve the assessment, then (2) the structural levers, of which BOCES back-office (~$95-185/yr if fully passed through) is the most realistic and least political — but be honest that it's two-figures-to-low-three-figures, not the headline.

**For the unincorporated-ToH homeowner only,** insert "push your town to absorb the commissioner sanitary district (mid-hundreds/yr where the 3x gap is real)" ahead of the structural levers — the one place the lead's #1 actually earns its ranking.
