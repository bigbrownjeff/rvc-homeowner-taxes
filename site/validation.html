The Big Four — errors that would have been fatal in Albany

1 · "Exemptions cost the schools $6.9M a year" — wrong kind of money

New York school districts levy a fixed dollar amount. Exemptions don't reduce district revenue — they shift the levy onto non-exempt neighbors (NYS Comptroller: "Every exemption granted on a property shifts the tax burden to the non-exempt properties."). And STAR — the largest piece of the claimed gap — is reimbursed by the State (RPTL §1306-a); it costs the district $0. The "$688K–$3.4M recaptured school revenue" scenarios mislabel taxpayer relief and state savings as district revenue, and the "deficit coverage 18–91%" table compares categories that can't be added. The deck rebuilds this math honestly (page 5) — and the honest version is still persuasive.

2 · The §467 senior-exemption table was shifted $8,400 — and is outdated

The project claimed 50% off for incomes up to $58,399, sliding to 5% at $66,799. Per Nassau's official brochure: 50% requires income ≤ $50,000; $58,399 is the ceiling of the minimum 5% tier; no higher tier exists. Since Dec 2025 (Ch. 581), the scale actually runs 5–65% (65% for income ≤ $47,000), and the statutory income cap rises to $75,000 on July 1, 2027. Bonus implication: RVC's senior median income (~$77–80K) means the median senior homeowner doesn't qualify for §467 at all — which breaks the project's "1,404 parcels × $4,900" arithmetic (see #4).

3 · "Prop 19-style portability for NY" — solving a California problem New York doesn't have

California needs portability because Prop 13 taxes acquisition value — selling re-bases you upward. New York taxes current assessed value (RPTL §305): there is no basis to port, Nassau sales don't trigger reassessment (the roll is frozen for a 6th straight year), and a downsizing senior's taxes already fall with the cheaper home. The state-level centerpiece was rebuilt on NY-native vehicles: S3309/A5288 (keep your exemption in the year you move — out of the Aging Committee 7–0), a senior tax-deferral enabling act (Oregon/Massachusetts/Washington/Texas precedents), and S3574 (renewal-notice protection).

4 · The story was stale: the deficit is already closed — by cutting classrooms

The documents treat the $3.78M deficit as looming. It ended: the board adopted a $141,323,369 budget (+1.03%) on Apr 16, 2026, eliminating ≈22 teaching positions and 40 teaching assistants, and voters passed it 1,915–1,195 on May 19, 2026 (levy +2.06%, under the final ≈2.1% cap — the "2.45%/$111.9M" figure was January's superseded projection). This is better ammunition than a projection: the cuts are what "do nothing about housing turnover" looks like. (Also: Mary Beth Joyce, the cited budget analyst, won a board seat in that same election.)

Framing fixes (concepts, not just digits)

ReplaceWith
"Recaptured school tax revenue"; "deficit coverage"Burden-shift relief to other taxpayers + state savings. The only channels that add district revenue from turnover: the tax-base growth factor (buyer renovations physically expand the taxable base, lawfully raising the levy cap), enrollment-linked aid, and budget-vote sustainability.
Enhanced STAR counted in district losses ($2,680/parcel)STAR is a state charge. A senior→family transition saves NY State ≈$2,058/yr (Enhanced credit $3,147 → Basic $1,089) — the "downsizing dividend" that can fund the incentives.
"Seniors lose §467/STAR benefits if they move" (lock-in driver)Mostly myth: Enhanced STAR follows an eligible owner to any NY primary residence; §467 applies wherever adopted; Nassau waives its 12-month wait for prior holders. The real frictions: move-year timing (S3309 fixes it), capital gains, inventory.
"STAR credit conversion shifts $2,680/parcel from local burden to state"The exemption is already state-funded; conversion changes payment plumbing and lets the benefit grow ≤2%/yr — good for the homeowner, neutral for the levy.
"1,404 exempt senior parcels (18% of owner homes)"2,076 senior-owned homes (27.9% — ACS B25007), of which the §467-eligible subset is unknown until the county publishes parcel data. Never multiply every senior home by a full benefit stack.
"288 seats of spare capacity"A ~260–290-student enrollment decline (NYSED: 3,533→3,276). No public capacity study exists; say "empty seats" only in that bounded sense.
Marginal cost = $0 (plan) and $5K one-time (model) and $12K/yr (same model)One labeled assumption: low marginal cost while spare seats last, rising as they fill. (The model's own ratio row said new students cost 3× the "recaptured" revenue — quietly contradicting its headline.)

Unverifiable as published — label as estimates, or go get the data

  • $15,230 average total tax bill and the bill-component split — plausible, but no public source; the cited Ownwell page shows a village-levy-only $1,465 (unusable).
  • How many RVC parcels actually hold §467 / Enhanced STAR, by tier — knowable only from county roll data / FOIL. Until then no aggregate "exemption gap" number should be published. This became Ask #3 of the deck.
  • RVC UFSD's own §467 adoption + ceiling + 65%-tier posture — supported only by county-wide statements; confirm with the district clerk.
  • "288 seats of spare capacity" (no facilities study); Riverside's "+$15K/student" (a resident's public comment); "$22M state aid"; "$15K/family retail spending"; "20–40 parcels per §467 tier"; Garden City's "$27M reserves".

What survives — the corrected case is still strong

The hypothesis holds; it just needed honest plumbing. Verified: the squeeze is real and now has a body count (62 classroom positions cut to balance 2026-27, with revenue growing ~1% against +$2M health insurance alone, and 61% of Nassau's 56 districts declining). The homes exist (2,076 senior-owned — more than the project assumed). The lock-in is real and fixable (§121 unindexed since 1997 with ~1.9M senior homes above the caps — AEI; benefit-timing traps — S3309 fixes; missing downsize inventory). And the honest fiscal upside is attractive: each transition saves the State ~$2,058/yr in STAR (which can fund the carrots), pays ~$3,300 in one-time transfer tax, relieves the §467 burden-shift on neighbors, grows the levy cap through renovations, and refills empty seats. Every ask has a live vehicle and a named, current officeholder — including two RVC residents (Rep. Gillen and Sen. Canzoneri-Fitzpatrick).
