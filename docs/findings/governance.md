# Nassau County's Overlapping Taxing Districts: Origins, Costs, and What's Recoverable

**Prepared June 10, 2026 · Public-finance research memo · Focus: school/education taxes, Rockville Centre (RVC) homeowner POV**

**Labeling convention:** **[VERIFIED]** = taken from a primary document or confirmed in 2+ independent sources. **[REPORTED]** = single credible secondary source. **[ESTIMATE]** = a study's projection or this memo's arithmetic, flagged as such.

---

## Executive Summary — for a homeowner reading their tax bill

**Where the layers come from.** Your bill is a geological record. The school district line dates to an 1812 state law that carved every town into tiny "common school districts" decades before villages or suburbs existed ([NYS Common School Law, June 19, 1812; Molloy College LI education timeline](https://www.molloy.edu/about/community-outreach/history-of-long-island/education)). The village line dates mostly to the 1910s–30s, when communities incorporated to control zoning and services. The sanitation/water/park lines date to special 1920s legislation written for Nassau alone (Ch. 516, Laws of 1928), which let elected commissioners run districts — a structure abolished statewide in 1932 but grandfathered in Nassau ([OSC, *Town Special Districts in New York*, 2007](https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/publications/pdf/townspecialdistricts.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**. None of these lines were drawn to match each other, which is why ~20% of Village of Rockville Centre residents are in the *Oceanside* school district and some RVC UFSD taxpayers live outside the village ([RVC Board of Trustees statement, Feb. 22, 2010](http://www.rvcny.us/new/BoT%20on%20RVCSD%202-22-10.pdf); [rvcny.gov "About"](https://www.rvcny.gov/about)) **[VERIFIED]**.

**What the overlap costs.** Schools are the largest line (~60%+ of a typical Nassau bill) ([PTRC, 2026](https://ptrc.com/blog/nassau-county-2026-general-tax-rates-explained-what-goes-into-your-property-tax-bill); [Nassau County, "How Is My Property Tax Determined"](https://www.nassaucountyny.gov/3754/How-Is-My-Property-Tax-Determined)). Nassau runs 56 school districts ([Nassau BOCES](https://www.nassauboces.org/schooldistrictdirectory)) — 56 superintendents (average LI superintendent pay >$243,000, NYSED data via [Patch](https://patch.com/new-york/massapequa/long-island-school-superintendent-salaries-ranked)) **[VERIFIED]** — plus 64 villages, 3 towns, 2 cities, and roughly 240 special districts ([Newsday mapping project via Patch, 2011](https://patch.com/new-york/farmingdale/bp--nassau-countys-special-districts-and-service-prov09e5120bee)). Nassau's town special districts alone took in ~$400M ($946/household, 2004) — 52% of all town revenue, the highest share in the state ([OSC, 2007](https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/publications/pdf/townspecialdistricts.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**. Long Island plans the highest per-pupil spending of any NY region: $37,758 (2024-25 budgets) vs. $33,404 statewide ([Empire Center, 2024](https://www.empirecenter.org/publications/school-budgets-outpace-inflation-as-districts-plan-to-spend-over-33k-per-student/)) **[VERIFIED]**.

**What's plausibly recoverable.** Honest answer: meaningful but bounded. The credible, sourced opportunities are (1) special-district reform — the Nassau Comptroller priced it at **$23.8–35.7M/yr** county-wide plus **$18M/yr** in Hempstead sanitation alone (Lundine Commission, 2008, citing Weitzman) **[ESTIMATE]**; (2) school back-office/shared services via BOCES — the model the Lundine Commission and LI Index favored over full mergers; (3) assessment de-duplication (145 NY villages still run parallel assessments; Lundine, 2008); and (4) ending the structural costs of Nassau's unique "county guarantee" refund machine (county paid ~$89M in certiorari refunds/claims in FY2024 alone; [NIFA staff report, Aug. 2025](https://nifa.ny.gov/system/files/documents/2025/08/nifa-staff-report-8-7-25.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**. Full school-district consolidation savings are real for *small* districts (Duncombe-Yinger, 2007) but Nassau's districts average ~3,700 pupils — past the steep part of the cost curve — so a county-wide district's defensible savings are low-to-mid single digits of operating cost **[ESTIMATE]**, and every Long Island merger attempt on record has been voted down or rebuffed.

### 1. ORIGINS: Why the boundaries look like this

**The school grid predates everything else.**
- **1812/1814 Common School Acts.** The Common School Law of June 19, 1812 directed that *each township be divided into common school districts* and tied state money to the structure; an 1814 amendment made the system compulsory by conditioning aid on local participation. Common districts were small by design (walking distance) and were not authorized to operate high schools ([Molloy College LI education timeline](https://www.molloy.edu/about/community-outreach/history-of-long-island/education); [NYSED, *School District Reorganization: A Primer*, July 2024](https://www.nysed.gov/sites/default/files/programs/regionalization/school-district-reorganization-a-primer_7.18.2024.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**. New York eventually had **over 10,000 districts**, now ~700 ([NYSED Primer, 2024](https://www.nysed.gov/sites/default/files/programs/regionalization/school-district-reorganization-a-primer_7.18.2024.pdf)); the Lundine Commission counted **685** in 2008 ([21st Century Local Government, 2008](https://www.cgr.org/consensuscny/docs/NYS_LGEC_Report.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **1853 Union Free districts** — "unions" of common districts, "free" of the ban on running high schools — are the template for most Nassau districts (hence "UFSD" on your bill) ([NYSED Primer, 2024](https://www.nysed.gov/sites/default/files/programs/regionalization/school-district-reorganization-a-primer_7.18.2024.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **1914/1925 centralization laws** created Central School Districts with state aid sweeteners; the first central school organized in 1914 ([NYSED district superintendency history](https://www.p12.nysed.gov/ds/history.html)) **[VERIFIED]**. This is the consolidation wave that swept *rural upstate* — thousands of one-room districts merged into centrals through the 1930s–50s.
- **Why it stalled on Long Island.** Timing. Upstate centralization was driven by sparse population and the lure of aid. Nassau's districts suburbanized *before* they could be centralized: Levittown alone added 17,000+ homes by 1951 ([OSC, 2007](https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/publications/pdf/townspecialdistricts.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**. Once a union-free district had its own high school, tax base, and identity, the incentive to merge vanished. 1950s statutes allowed common districts to fold into small city districts, and "by the 1960s the wave of consolidation was essentially over" ([Molloy timeline](https://www.molloy.edu/about/community-outreach/history-of-long-island/education)) **[REPORTED]**. Nassau's notable mid-century merger: **North Shore CSD (1953)** from the Sea Cliff, Glen Head, and Glenwood Landing districts ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Shore_Central_School_District)) **[VERIFIED]**. A telling artifact: **all three of New York State's central *high school* districts are in Nassau** (Valley Stream CHSD, Bellmore-Merrick CHSD, Sewanhaka CHSD) — half-consolidations in which separate elementary districts share a secondary district. Valley Stream's was formed by petition in **February 1925**, with a 9-member board appointed 3-each by elementary Districts 13, 24, and 30 ([VSCHSD district history](https://vschsd.org/district/district-history/); [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valley_Stream_Central_High_School_District)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **Race and class dynamics are documented, not speculative.** Lakeview (Black, shaped by postwar redlining) vs. white Malverne/North Lynbrook produced the state's first desegregation order: families petitioned Education Commissioner James E. Allen Jr. beginning in 1963; his Princeton Plan order was fought to the U.S. Supreme Court (which let desegregation stand, 1965); busing wasn't fully implemented until 1978 ([History News Network](https://historynewsnetwork.org/article/182496); [LI Herald](https://www.liherald.com/stories/getting-past-malverne-districts-past,119970); [CBS New York](https://www.cbsnews.com/newyork/news/malverne-school-desegregation-black-history-month-fred-brewington-dr-martin-luther-king-jr/)) **[VERIFIED]**. ERASE Racism finds LI's "fragmented, geographically small school districts… institutionalize residential segregation": intensely segregated (90–100% nonwhite) districts more than doubled 2004–2016, and such districts average nearly **$10,000 less revenue per student** than predominantly white districts ([ERASE Racism, *Heading in the Wrong Direction*, 2015](https://www.eraseracismny.org/home/1-resource/360-heading-in-the-wrong-direction-growing-school-segregation-on-long-island); [ERASE Racism district facts](http://eraseracismny.org/storage/documents/education/ERASE_Racism-long-island-district-facts.pdf); see also [Century Foundation, *Housing and Educational Inequality: The Case of Long Island*](https://tcf.org/content/report/housing-and-educational-inequality-the-case-of-long-island/)) **[VERIFIED]**.

**The special-district lattice is a Nassau-specific legal fossil.**
- Statewide town improvement districts date to 1909 (Ch. 63); **Nassau got its own enabling act in 1928 (Ch. 516, Laws of 1928)** providing for *elected boards of commissioners*. When the modern Town Law (Ch. 634, Laws of 1932) abolished separate commissioner boards statewide, older districts — "primarily in Nassau County" — were **grandfathered by referendum** ([OSC, 2007](https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/publications/pdf/townspecialdistricts.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**. The Lundine Commission, quoting Nassau Comptroller Howard Weitzman: "In 1932, state legislation was enacted that… many Nassau commissioner-run districts were maintained," leaving Nassau "the only county with significant numbers of such districts" — **49 commissioner-run town tax districts, plus 41 fire districts and 38 library districts** ([Lundine report, 2008, p.34](https://www.cgr.org/consensuscny/docs/NYS_LGEC_Report.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **The 1938 county charter** made Nassau an outlier in the other direction: Nassau is one of only **two county assessing units in New York** (with Tompkins) — the county, not the towns, assesses all property ([Lundine report, 2008: "1,128 individual assessing units… two county assessing units… 145 villages which assess… a duplicative function"](https://www.cgr.org/consensuscny/docs/NYS_LGEC_Report.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**. The charter package also created the **"county guarantee"**: because the county controls the roll, the county (not the school district or town) is liable to refund over-assessments *including the school and town shares* — see §5 below for what that costs.
- **Why school lines ≠ village lines (the RVC example).** They were never the same system: school districts are state creatures drawn in the 1800s; the Village of Rockville Centre incorporated in 1893 around a railroad commercial core. The Village Board of Trustees put it plainly in a February 22, 2010 public statement: "the Village of Rockville Centre and the Rockville Centre School District are not only separate governmental entities, they also have different boundaries. Most Village residents live in the RVC School District, but others live in the Oceanside or Baldwin school districts… Conversely, some residents of the RVC School District do not live in the Village and so pay no property taxes here" ([RVC BoT statement, 2010](http://www.rvcny.us/new/BoT%20on%20RVCSD%202-22-10.pdf)) **[VERIFIED — primary]**. The village's own site puts the Oceanside-SD share of village residents at ~20%, with a few streets in Baldwin SD ([rvcny.gov](https://www.rvcny.gov/about)) **[REPORTED]**. The same 2010 document records the two governments billing *each other* — the village began charging the district ~$40,000/yr for garbage pickup; the district threatened to evict the village summer-rec program from school buildings — a perfect miniature of inter-layer friction costs **[VERIFIED]**.

### 2. REDUNDANCIES TODAY: the layers on one bill

A Nassau homeowner gets **two main bills** (general tax — county/town/special districts; school tax — district + library), plus a **separate village bill** if in one of the 64 villages ([Nassau County](https://www.nassaucountyny.gov/3754/How-Is-My-Property-Tax-Determined)) **[VERIFIED]**. The stack:

| Layer | RVC (village) homeowner | Unincorporated Town of Hempstead homeowner |
|---|---|---|
| County general + county police HQ | ✔ | ✔ |
| County police *district* | ✘ (village has own PD, 58 officers — [LI Press, 2026](https://www.longislandpress.com/2026/05/06/rockville-centre-village-family/)) | ✔ (NCPD patrol) |
| Town of Hempstead general | ✔ | ✔ + part-town funds |
| Village tax (roads, sanitation, parks, water, **municipal electric**) | ✔ ([rvcny.gov](https://www.rvcny.gov/departments); [NYS DPS](https://dps.ny.gov/location/village-rockville-centre-electric)) | ✘ |
| Town special districts: sanitation/refuse, water, park, lighting, sewer collection | ✘ (village provides) | ✔ — one of 24 refuse/garbage, 28 water, 23 park, 31 fire-protection etc. town districts ([OSC, 2007, 2004 data: Nassau total 140](https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/publications/pdf/townspecialdistricts.pdf)) |
| Fire district / fire protection | village fire dept via village tax | ✔ (41 fire districts county-wide; Lundine 2008) |
| School district (RVC UFSD — or Oceanside/Baldwin!) | ✔ largest line | ✔ |
| Library district | ✔ | ✔ (≈54 libraries/library districts; [NCLA](https://ncla.info/)) |
| County sewage disposal districts | ✔ | ✔ |

**Where functions duplicate — with sources:**
- **Assessment, three times.** The county assesses (1938 charter); **villages that opted to remain assessing units run a parallel roll for the same parcels** (RPTL §1402 options, [tax.ny.gov](https://www.tax.ny.gov/research/property/legal/localop/village.htm)); Lundine counted 145 such villages statewide and called village assessing "a duplicative function" ([2008](https://www.cgr.org/consensuscny/docs/NYS_LGEC_Report.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**. A homeowner can grieve the county roll *and* separately grieve the village roll.
- **56 school central offices.** 56 superintendents, HR, payroll, purchasing, and transportation departments for a county of ~420K parcels. LI superintendent average pay >$243K (NYSED disclosure data, [Patch](https://patch.com/new-york/massapequa/long-island-school-superintendent-salaries-ranked)); **57 of New York's 100 highest-paid public-school educators worked on LI** ([Empire Center](https://www.empirecenter.org/publications/57-li-school-officials-among-nys-highest-paid-public-educators/)) **[VERIFIED]**. The Valley Stream community alone supports **four** school boards/administrations (Districts 13, 24, 30 + the CHSD) ([VSCHSD](https://vschsd.org/district/district-information/)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **Police.** NCPD plus ~19 village/special-district police forces (e.g., Port Washington Police District; RVC PD) ([516update guide](https://516update.com/p/long-island-police); [Wikipedia list](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_law_enforcement_agencies_on_Long_Island)) **[VERIFIED]**. Village PD residents are excluded from the county police-district levy but still fund NCPD headquarters functions — two command structures per acre.
- **Sanitation.** Nassau's 24 garbage districts (16 commissioner-run) hold **15% of the state's sanitation districts but ~50% of statewide sanitation-district costs**; Hempstead commissioner-district bills ran **up to 3× higher** than comparable town-run service ([Lundine, 2008](https://www.cgr.org/consensuscny/docs/NYS_LGEC_Report.pdf); [OSC, 2007, citing Nassau Comptroller audits](https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/publications/pdf/townspecialdistricts.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **Water.** 28 separate town water districts (2004 count) sit beside village systems (RVC runs its own water + electric) and, until 2022, an investor-owned utility (NY American Water → Liberty) whose customers also paid the utility's property taxes inside their rates ([OSC, 2007](https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/publications/pdf/townspecialdistricts.pdf); [Gov. Cuomo PSC study release, 2021](https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-cuomo-announces-results-new-york-american-water-study-together-plan-avert-looming-rate)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **The aggregate:** LI-wide, **665 government entities including 168 special districts** ([Rauch Foundation / LI Index](https://www.rauchfoundation.org/news-resources/archive/long-island-must-shed-small-town-mind-set/)); ~**240** fire, sanitation, water, library, parks, parking, police, school and sewer districts mapped in Nassau alone ([Newsday project via Patch, 2011](https://patch.com/new-york/farmingdale/bp--nassau-countys-special-districts-and-service-prov09e5120bee)); Nassau special districts = **2% of NY districts but 31% of all district revenues**, $400M levy / $946 per household / **51.8% of total town revenues** (2004) ([OSC, 2007](https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/publications/pdf/townspecialdistricts.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**.

### 3. SAVINGS ESTIMATES: what credible studies actually found

**Academic benchmark — Duncombe & Yinger (Syracuse), "Does School District Consolidation Cut Costs?" (*Education Finance & Policy*, 2007), NY rural-district panel:**
- Doubling enrollment cuts **operating** costs per pupil by **61.7%** for a 300-pupil district and **49.6%** for a 1,500-pupil district; after large **adjustment costs** (especially capital), **net** savings fall to **31.5%** and **14.4%** respectively. Conclusion: consolidation "makes fiscal sense, particularly for very small districts," but states "should avoid subsidizing unwarranted capital projects" ([ERIC EJ896695](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ896695); [Syracuse SURFACE](https://surface.syr.edu/cpr/122/)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- Implication for Nassau: with average district enrollment ~3,700 (LI Index figure below), most Nassau districts are *past* the steep portion of the cost curve; the same authors' 1994 NY study found "relatively few New York districts are strong candidates for full consolidation, although some may benefit from sharing of administrative and support functions" ([Duncombe, Miner & Ruggiero 1994, ERIC ED395747](https://eric.ed.gov/?q=shed&pg=359&id=ED395747); [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/027277579400011T)) **[VERIFIED]**. Literature consensus (AASA review): diseconomies appear in very large districts; savings concentrate under ~1,500–2,000 pupils ([AASA, *School District Consolidation: The Benefits and Costs*](https://www.aasa.org/resources/resource/school-district-consolidation-the-benefits-and-costs)) **[REPORTED]**.

**Lundine Commission (NYS Commission on Local Government Efficiency & Competitiveness, April 2008, "21st Century Local Government"):**
- Identified "**more than $1 billion**" in recurring savings from the subset of recommendations it could quantify ([report](https://www.cgr.org/consensuscny/docs/NYS_LGEC_Report.pdf), p.2) **[VERIFIED]**; press summaries round to $1.1B ([NYS Archives finding aid](https://findingaids.nysed.gov/do/0db08290-45f9-5176-b13f-60bae79eecd0)) **[REPORTED]**.
- School recommendations: empower the Commissioner of Education to **order** consolidation; local school restructuring committees; **regional collective bargaining for new hires**; consolidate **back-office services and regional high schools** **[VERIFIED]**.
- Nassau-specific: adopt town takeover of commissioner-run sanitation (**$18M/yr savings in Town of Hempstead alone**, per Weitzman analysis); the Nassau Comptroller's "Cost-Savings Ideas for Special Districts in Nassau County" estimated **$23.8M–$35.7M** in district savings (insurance pooling, joint purchasing); end commissioner pay/benefits (one board member retiring at 55 ≈ **$500,000** lifetime health cost, Nassau Comptroller via Newsday 4/11/2007) **[VERIFIED — as estimates within a primary report]** **[ESTIMATE as to realization]**.
- Assessment: county-level assessing; Erie County Comptroller study pegged county-wide assessment savings at **$2.1–3.9M** for Erie ([Lundine, 2008](https://www.cgr.org/consensuscny/docs/NYS_LGEC_Report.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**.

**Long Island Index / Rauch Foundation:**
- *A Tale of Two Suburbs* (LI Index comparative study with northern Virginia, 2006–07 cycle): Fairfax/Loudoun run **17 governmental units vs. Long Island's 239** (incl. **127 school districts averaging 3,735 students**); LI had **13% more government employees** and spent **~83% more on total salaries**; NoVA residents reported equal or higher service satisfaction and equal access to school boards (49% both regions) ([Washington Examiner summary by Chris Braunlich, May 21, 2007](https://www.washingtonexaminer.com/chris-braunlich-long-islanders-pay-more-but-get-more-than-northern-virginians); [Rauch archive](https://www.rauchfoundation.org/news-resources/archive/long-island-must-shed-small-town-mind-set/)) **[VERIFIED]**. The Rauch "About School Costs" analysis: LI spent **$17,392 per pupil — $5,369 (45%) more than northern Virginia**, equal to **$834 per resident**; LI's largest districts (avg 7,802 students) cost **$16,793/pupil** vs. **$21,183 (+26%)** in the smallest ([Rauch Foundation archive](https://rauchfoundation.org/news-resources/archive/about-school-costs/), figures as indexed; mid-2000s dollars) **[REPORTED — archive page, figures not re-verified in original PDF]**.
- *LI Index 2009 education study* (released Jan. 30, 2009; Hofstra, Fiscal Policy Institute, Stony Brook Survey Center): the region's "large number of small districts" causes "inefficiency and inflexibility, and a mismatch between school resources and student needs"; floated NoVA-style county systems, Minnesota commercial-tax-base pooling (73% LI support in polling), magnet/inter-district options — but published **no single consolidation dollar figure** ([Schott Foundation summary](https://schottfoundation.org/the-latest/long-island-index-education-study-finds-inefficiencies-and-inequalities-mismatch-in-funding-versus-student-needs/)) **[VERIFIED as to findings; no savings number exists to quote]**.

**BOCES as quasi-consolidation (the live mechanism):**
- Nassau BOCES provides shared services to all **56** districts ([Nassau BOCES](https://www.nassauboces.org/schooldistrictdirectory)). State BOCES aid reimburses districts for shared-service costs at an aid ratio with a **36% floor and 90% ceiling** (wealthy Nassau districts sit near the floor); statewide, BOCES admin/capital is ~40% state-aided and service agreements ~21% ([NYSSBA BOCES budget explainer, June 2025](https://www.nyssba.org/news/2025/06/06/on-board-online-june-9-2025/what-every-local-school-board-member-should-know-about-the-boces-budget/); [school-finance survey](https://schoolfinancesdav.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/32-new-york.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**. The 2025 state budget doubled the aidable BOCES salary cap **$30,000 → $60,000**, phased 2026-27 through 2028-29 — a deliberate expansion of the shared-services channel ([BOCES of NYS](https://www.boces.org/new-york-state-budget-includes-long-awaited-changes-to-modernize-boces-aid/)) **[VERIFIED]**.

**State reorganization incentive aid (the carrot nobody on LI takes):**
- Merging districts get **Reorganization Incentive Operating Aid: a 40% bonus on the Foundation Aid base for years 1–5, declining 4 points/yr, ending year 15** (i.e., up to 14 years of extra aid), plus a **~30% building-aid bump** for reorganization-related projects ([NYSED Primer, July 2024](https://www.nysed.gov/sites/default/files/programs/regionalization/school-district-reorganization-a-primer_7.18.2024.pdf); [NYSED state-aid chart](https://stateaid.nysed.gov/build/html_docs/reorgincval_080808.htm); [RBT CPAs, 2025](https://www.rbtcpas.com/thought-leadership-articles/education/to-merge-or-not-to-merge-new-york-state-offering-increased-aid-to-consolidating-school-districts/)) **[VERIFIED]**. Caveat for Nassau: the bonus rides on *Foundation Aid*, which is small for wealthy districts — so the carrot is structurally weakest exactly where the districts are smallest and richest **[ESTIMATE/analysis]**.

**Documented LI merger attempts — all failed:**
- **Tuckahoe–Southampton (Suffolk), 2013–14:** feasibility study commissioned; Southampton voters defeated the straw vote (Oct. 2013), ~150 students staged a protest sit-in; a second attempt **failed at the polls in 2014**. Driver: Southampton's low tax rate would rise to absorb Tuckahoe ([27east coverage](https://www.27east.com/southampton-press/southampton-students-protest-failed-merger-vote-2-1439429/), [merger-fails report](http://www.27east.com/news/article.cfm/Southampton-Village-Surrounding-Areas/87998/Tuckahoe-And-Southampton-Residents-Head-To-Polls-Over-Merger)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **Elwood (Suffolk), 2010–11:** sent merger proposals to Northport-East Northport, Half Hollow Hills, Commack, South Huntington, and Harborfields; **none accepted** ([Wikipedia: Elwood UFSD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwood_Union_Free_School_District)) **[REPORTED]**.
- **NYSED "regionalization" initiative, 2024–25:** a BOCES-facilitated regional planning rule (not even merger) triggered a Long Island political revolt — county officials, the LI Senate delegation, and school boards branded it a "takeover"; Hochul declared participation should be optional; Martins/Blumencranz introduced the "Our Schools, Our Rules Act" to ban state-mandated regionalization ([NBC New York, Nov. 2024](https://www.nbcnewyork.com/new-york/regionalization-plan-schools-long-island/6008542/); [NY Senate GOP release](https://www.nysenate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2024/steven-d-rhoads/members-long-island-senate-republican-conference-local); [Northport Journal](https://northportjournal.com/schools/nysed-regionalization-initiative-goes-public-officials-respond-to-community-concerns)) **[VERIFIED]**.

**Counter-evidence — why savings are routinely overstated:**
- **Adjustment costs**: D-Y found capital "adjustment" spending consumes roughly half of gross savings (above) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **Salary leveling-up**: merged districts harmonize contracts to the higher schedule; NYSUT's merger fact sheet and the NYSSBA consolidation brief both flag wage harmonization and transition costs as first-order offsets ([NYSUT Fact Sheet 25-3](https://www.nysut.org/resources/all-listing/research/fact-sheets/fact-sheet-school-district-mergers-consolidations); [NYSSBA issue brief](https://www.nyssba.org/clientuploads/nyssba_pdf/GR-SIC-School-District-Consolidation-Issue-Brief.pdf)) **[REPORTED]**.
- **Identity/local control**: the 2009 LI Index polling found Long Islanders *like* small-district responsiveness; competition among districts is argued to spur quality ([Molloy timeline](https://www.molloy.edu/about/community-outreach/history-of-long-island/education)) **[REPORTED]**. Home-value capitalization of district brand is widely believed and drives merger opposition (Tuckahoe coverage), though LI-specific hedonic estimates were not located in this review **[flagged, not quantified]**.
- **Privatization analogue**: Michigan food-service privatization studies found labor savings (~15%) fully offset by contractor fees (+11%) and supplies (+4%) — i.e., outsourcing ≠ automatic savings ([In the Public Interest / U-M studies](https://inthepublicinterest.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/PrivatizedSchoolFoodServiceAndStudentPerformance-Michigan.pdf)) **[REPORTED]**.

### 4. WHAT-IFS, sketched honestly

**(a) County-wide or town-wide school district (Maryland/Virginia model).**
- Scale logic: one district of ~190K students (county-wide) or three town-scale districts of ~40–80K. The LI Index NoVA comparison is the best available bound: **45% lower per-pupil spending in county-run NoVA** — but that gap bundles regional wages, pensions, and program differences, *not* just governance; treat it as a ceiling, not a projection ([Rauch archive](https://rauchfoundation.org/news-resources/archive/about-school-costs/)) **[ESTIMATE]**.
- A defensible *administration-only* range: eliminating ~50 of 56 central offices touches superintendents (~$13–14M/yr in supt. compensation alone at the >$243K LI average × 56) plus assistant-supt/business-office multiples; LI Index's own size gradient (smallest districts +26% per-pupil vs. largest) and D-Y's flattening curve suggest **roughly 2–7% of operating cost** is realistically addressable for mid-size suburban districts — single-digit, not transformative, and partially consumed by leveling-up and transition costs ([Rauch](https://rauchfoundation.org/news-resources/archive/about-school-costs/); [Duncombe-Yinger 2007](https://surface.syr.edu/cpr/122/); [Thompson dissertation, 2014, ERIC ED556985](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=ED556985) — concludes "plausibility of a regional merger… is high" on finance/enrollment grounds but offers no dollar figure) **[ESTIMATE]**.
- What's lost: hyper-local boards (the 2024-25 regionalization revolt shows the politics), perceived district-brand home-value premium, and — per ERASE Racism — *possibly gained*: desegregation and tax-base equity, which is precisely why boundary politics are fierce ([ERASE Racism, 2015](https://www.eraseracismny.org/home/1-resource/360-heading-in-the-wrong-direction-growing-school-segregation-on-long-island)) **[VERIFIED context]**.

**(b) Back-office / transportation / purchasing consolidation (BOCES + CWSSI).**
- This is the path with realized, audited numbers. Under the 2017 **County-Wide Shared Services Initiative**, Nassau's panel reported the **highest first-year savings in the state: $130.5M (2018)** — dominated by one project, consolidation of **Nassau County and City of Long Beach wastewater treatment (projected >$128M; realized savings to county and city exceeded $100M**, ultimately funded such that it fell out of state match eligibility) ([Rockefeller Institute review of CWSSI plans](https://rockinst.org/issue-area/review-plans-submitted-state-county-wide-shared-services-initiative-2/); [Nassau 2022-23 Shared Services Plan](https://rockinst.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/02/12-21-22-Nassau-Shared-Services-Report-2022-23.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**. Subsequent Nassau plans are incremental (joint purchasing, IT, water-district cooperation); the state paid **~$1M in matching funds to two Nassau villages** for 2022 savings ([NY DOS, 2023](https://dos.ny.gov/news/new-york-department-state-announces-nearly-1-million-state-matching-funds-awarded-two-nassau)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- School back-office via BOCES is the LI-feasible version of consolidation: aid-subsidized (36–90% ratios), no boundary change, now expanded by the $60K salary-cap change ([NYSSBA, 2025](https://www.nyssba.org/news/2025/06/06/on-board-online-june-9-2025/what-every-local-school-board-member-should-know-about-the-boces-budget/)) **[VERIFIED]**. Magnitude: if shared business functions trim even 1–2% of Nassau's ~$8B+ aggregate school spending, that is $80–160M/yr — consistent with the Lundine framing that back-office and special-district reform, not mergers, are where LI money is **[ESTIMATE — this memo's arithmetic]**.

**(c) Deconsolidation — it still happens, and it costs.**
- **Mastic Beach (Suffolk)**: incorporated 2010 over town-service complaints; faced a budget gap requiring a ~125% spending increase (~$250/home); voters dissolved it **1,922–1,215 in Nov. 2016** (effective 2017), accepting a 22% Brookhaven tax bump as the cheaper option — the clearest LI-region price tag on adding a government layer ([Long Island Press, 2016](https://www.longislandpress.com/2016/11/17/mastic-beach-votes-to-dissolve-making-it-third-shortest-lived-village-in-ny/); [CBS NY](https://www.cbsnews.com/newyork/news/mastic-beach-village-vote/)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- The 2009 **Government Reorganization & Citizen Empowerment Act** (eff. March 2010) lets voters force dissolution referenda with signatures of **10% of voters or 5,000, whichever is less** ([NY DOS](https://dos.ny.gov/new-ny-government-reorganization-and-citizen-empowerment-act)) **[VERIFIED]**; the **Citizen Empowerment Tax Credit** pays merging/dissolving governments **15% of the combined levy annually (capped $1M), ≥70% to direct tax relief** ([Governor's office, 2018](https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-cuomo-announces-97-million-delivered-local-municipalities-dissolving-duplicative)) **[VERIFIED]**. No Nassau village has dissolved under the Act; upstate examples (Altmar: $31,290 direct savings + $58K/yr CETC ≈ 45% village-tax cut) show the mechanics work at small scale ([DOS guidance](https://dos.ny.gov/system/files/documents/2024/05/reorganization-of-local-government.pdf)) **[REPORTED]**.
- Island Park residents have publicly explored village dissolution ([LI Herald](https://www.liherald.com/stories/saying-goodbye-to-island-park,32387)) **[REPORTED]**.

**(d) Public-private partnerships in NY K-12 — what's legal, what's not, what saved money.**
- **Legal and routine:** *Pupil transportation contracting* — contractors carry **>63% of NY's yellow-bus riders**; industry-claimed savings of 10–20% vs. district-run fleets (advocate figure, treat skeptically) ([NYSBCA](https://www.nysbca.com/Contracts); [First Student](https://firststudentinc.com/resources/articles/get-answers-to-frequently-asked-questions-like-how-can-contracting-transportation-services-save-my-district-money-and-others/); NYSED contract rules: [p12.nysed.gov](https://p12.nysed.gov/schoolbus/contracts.html)) **[VERIFIED as to legality/scale; ESTIMATE as to savings]**. *Energy performance contracts (ESCOs)*: NY schools executed **>$600M of EPCs in a decade**; an OSC 2017 regional audit of five districts (incl. **Manhasset UFSD**, guaranteed **$7.2M** life-of-contract savings; Island Trees; Half Hollow Hills; $52.5M total projects approved 2009-11) found districts often **could not verify guaranteed savings were achieved** — PPP works only with measurement teeth ([OSC EPC audit, 2017](https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/audits/2017-11/lgsa-audit-swr-2015-EPC-global.pdf); [Manhasset letter](https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/audits/2017-11/lgsa-audit-swr-2015-EPC-manhasset-school.pdf); [Ameresco](https://www.ameresco.com/sustainable-facility-win-win-new-york-schools/)) **[VERIFIED]**. *Food-service management companies*: permitted under NYSED child-nutrition rules; evidence of net savings is weak (Michigan studies above) **[REPORTED]**.
- **Not legal / not available:** private for-profit *operation* of district schools. The NY Constitution (Art. XI §1) obligates the legislature to maintain a system of "common schools, wherein all the children of this state may be educated," and Art. VIII §1's gift-and-loan clause constrains transferring public funds to private operators; charter schools (1998 Act) are *public* nonprofit corporations and a separate, capped channel — there is no Philadelphia-style private-management option for RVC UFSD **[VERIFIED as to framework; standard constitutional reading]**. School *facilities* monetization (sale-leasebacks to private operators) and toll-style concession PPPs likewise have no NY K-12 precedent on LI located in this review **[flagged]**.

### 5. OTHER AREAS FOR INVESTIGATION (flags)

- **The "county guarantee" / assessment-refund machine.** Uniquely, Nassau refunds over-assessments *including the school/town shares it merely collected*. At its peak the system generated **~$100M/yr in refunds ($30M residential, $70M commercial)**, drove **>$1.3B of borrowing** with ~$150M/yr debt service ([Nassau County news release, 2011, archive](https://archive.nassaucountyny.gov/agencies/CountyExecutive/NewsRelease/2010/4-14-2011.htm)); it helped trigger NIFA's creation (L. 2000, ch. 84); the 2016 **Disputed Assessment Fund** shifted commercial refund costs onto a levy-funded reserve ([Cullen & Dykman](https://www.cullenllp.com/blog/nassau-countys-new-disputed-assessment-fund-is-likely-to-raise-tax-rates/)); FY2024 the county still spent **~$89.2M on certiorari refunds and claims** ([NIFA staff report, Aug. 2025](https://nifa.ny.gov/system/files/documents/2025/08/nifa-staff-report-8-7-25.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**. The 2018 Curran reassessment → **200K+ protests, ~80K SCAR filings** in 2020-21 ([LI Uncovered, 2021](https://liuncoverednews.blogspot.com/2021/03/property-tax-refunds-back-to-future-in.html)) **[REPORTED]**. School districts are held harmless — which mutes their incentive to care about assessment accuracy.
- **Village/county double assessment.** 145 NY villages keep duplicate rolls (Lundine, 2008); Nassau villages may assess under RPTL §1402 — a candidate for painless elimination (villages can adopt the county roll) ([tax.ny.gov](https://www.tax.ny.gov/research/property/legal/localop/village.htm)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **Fire-district transparency.** OSC's fire-district accountability framework (independent audit required at ≥$400K revenue) exists because recurring audits find weak controls and misuse (e.g., Minetto: $22K misused) ([OSC fire-district guidance](https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/publications/pdf/arm-fds.pdf); [OSC, 2014](https://www.osc.ny.gov/press/releases/2014/12/dinapoli-audit-reveals-theft-cash-questionable-spending-minetto-volunteer-fire-dept-chief)); Nassau has 41 fire districts with elected commissioner boards and December low-turnout elections (Lundine, 2008) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **Water-district consolidation.** The PSC's 2021 statutory study found **municipalization of NY American Water's Nassau systems feasible and in the public interest under several scenarios**, recommended a new public authority and relief from the utility's property-tax burden (a major rate driver); the $607M sale to **Liberty Utilities** proceeded; Massapequa Water District found takeover of adjacent NYAW territory feasible ([Governor's release, 2021](https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-cuomo-announces-results-new-york-american-water-study-together-plan-avert-looming-rate); [LI Herald](https://www.liherald.com/stories/state-to-study-feasibility-of-taking-new-york-american-water-public,130090)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **Sanitation-district patronage.** Nassau Comptroller Weitzman's 2005 audits (SD-1, SD-2, SD-6, vs. well-run Port Washington) found excessive administrator pay, no-bid insurance, personal use of district vehicles, full-time pay for ~25-hour weeks, and "red flags for fraud"; commissioner elections draw turnout as low as **148 of 55,000 eligible** ([OSC, 2007, summarizing](https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/publications/pdf/townspecialdistricts.pdf); [OSC audit 2005-S-69 & 2016 follow-up](https://www.osc.ny.gov/files/local-government/audits/2017-11/lgsa-audit-special-district-2016-hempstead-sanitary-1_1.pdf); [CBS NY](https://www.cbsnews.com/newyork/news/nassau-sanitary-districts/)) **[VERIFIED]**.
