Rockville Centre · Housing × Schools BriefingJune 2026
A briefing for federal, state & county legislators

Unlock the Homes,
Keep the Teachers.

This spring, Rockville Centre balanced its school budget by cutting ≈22 teaching positions and 40 teaching assistants — in a village whose median household earns $151,938 and whose homes average $819,000.1,2,8 The problem isn't wealth. It's that the homes stopped changing hands: 27.9% of owner households are now headed by someone 65 or older,7 enrollment has fallen ~260 students in a decade,3 and the seniors who want to move face a wall of federal tax penalties, benefit-timing traps, and missing downsize inventory. Every lever below is carrots-only — no senior loses a benefit.

Three asks, each with a live vehicle

1

Federal — End the home-sale capital-gains lock-in

Cosponsor H.R. 1340 / S. 3332 (More Homes on the Market Act): double the §121 exclusion to $500K/$1M and index it — unchanged since 1997. AEI: ~1.9 million senior-owned homes are locked above today's caps.9,10

2

State — Make benefits move when seniors move, and let them defer

Pass S3309/A5288 (keeps a senior's exemption in the year of a move — out of Aging committee 7–0). Enact a senior property-tax deferral enabling act on the Oregon/Massachusetts model: taxes accrue against the home, paid in full at sale.11,12

3

County & local — Data before dollars

Publish parcel-level senior-exemption counts by school district before any opt-in to the new 65% exemption tier (Ch. 581 of 2025) or the $75,000 income cap arriving July 2027 — and fund a Silver-to-Gold downsizing pilot.13,14

What just happened in Rockville Centre's schools

Rockville Centre UFSD is an A-rated district — South Side High ranks #611 nationally, the graduation rate is 98–99% vs. New York's 87%.4 And in the 2026-27 budget cycle it had to choose between classrooms and solvency:

Jan 29, 2026 — District projects a $3.78M deficit for 2026-27: health insurance +10% (~$2M), out-of-district special-ed placements projected at $6.38M, total revenue growth only ~1% (~$1.4M).1

This is structural, not cyclical. Costs (health care, special education, salaries) compound faster than the ~2% levy cap plus ~15% state aid can grow. A district can cut its way through one year; it cannot cut its way through a decade. The 2026-27 cuts are what "do nothing about housing" looks like.

And RVC is the rule, not the exception

61% of Nassau's 56 school districts lost enrollment over the past decade (Nassau BOCES); Long Island public-school enrollment is down 6.3% since 2017.6 Hewlett-Woodmere cut 11 teaching positions in 2025-26; Oyster Bay-East Norwich is down 16.5% since 2015; Locust Valley projects 1,725 students by 2027-28, from 2,034 in 2018-19.6

The pipeline is inventory-starved, not demand-starved. Locust Valley's superintendent, explaining her district's enrollment slide: she could find five houses listed under $1 million in the entire district — "this is a Long Island problem, not a Locust Valley problem."6 Young families bid on the few large homes that list; the rest never list.
Why this is also the *least painful* fix. The alternative paths to school solvency are: override the tax cap (60% supermajority, on top of some of the nation's highest property taxes), cut programs year after year, or close a building. Housing turnover is the one lever that adds students, relieves taxpayers, and asks no current resident to give anything up.

Four locks on the front door

Surveyed seniors overwhelmingly say they'd consider downsizing — what stops the ones who want to go is concrete and fixable:

LockHow it worksThe fix
1 · Federal capital gainsThe §121 home-sale exclusion ($250K single / $500K joint) hasn't moved since 1997. A couple who bought in RVC in the 1990s for ~$250K and sells at ~$900K has taxable gain above the cap; a widowed senior filing single hits it twice as fast. AEI: ~1.9M senior-owned homes nationally sit on gains above the exclusions — $620B in locked value.9,10H.R. 1340 / S. 3332: double & index the exclusion. 117+ House cosponsors, bipartisan.11
2 · Benefit-timing trapsA senior who closes on a move at the wrong point in the assessment calendar can lose their senior exemption for the year — a real, documented penalty for moving. (The deeper fear — "I lose my STAR/§467 forever if I move" — is mostly misinformation; see below.)S3309/A5288 (Palumbo): apply within 30 days of closing, keep the exemption. Out of Aging 7–0, April 2026.12
3 · Cash-flow fearFixed-income seniors hoard equity against rising carrying costs and have no way to tap their tax bill's timing. Four states already solve this with deferral: the state pays the tax, a lien accrues at 5–8% simple interest, and the bill is settled in full at sale (OR · MA · WA · TX).13NY enabling act for senior school-tax deferral — district opt-in, zero long-run cost to the levy.
4 · Nothing to move intoRVC's housing stock is overwhelmingly single-family; the condo/ADU rungs of the downsizing ladder are missing, so "selling" means "leaving the village you've lived in for 40 years."Local: ADU policy, right-size zoning near the LIRR, senior-priority placement in new developments.
Myth-buster for senior constituents: Enhanced STAR follows you to any new NY primary residence (re-register, income-qualified). The §467 senior exemption applies at the new home wherever the local jurisdictions adopted it — and Nassau even waives its 12-month ownership wait for seniors who held the exemption before. Moving within New York does not forfeit your benefits — and because NY (unlike California) taxes current value, not purchase price, downsizing to a smaller home automatically lowers the tax bill.14
And the lock is tightening. Albany just authorized a 65% senior exemption tier (Ch. 581 of 2025, eff. 2026) and a $75,000 income ceiling from July 2027. Peer-reviewed evidence (NBER w25468: a century of Georgia exemptions) shows age-based property-tax relief measurably increases seniors' tendency to stay put. Relief without mobility deepens the freeze — every expansion should ship with a fiscal note and a mobility counterpart.15

What turnover is actually worth — by beneficiary

Briefings on this topic routinely claim senior exemptions "cost the schools millions" and that turnover "recaptures revenue." That framing is wrong, and we won't use it. New York school districts levy a fixed dollar amount: exemptions shift that levy onto non-exempt neighbors (NYS Comptroller: "Every exemption granted on a property shifts the tax burden to the non-exempt properties"), and STAR is reimbursed to districts by the State (RPTL §1306-a) — it costs districts nothing.16 Here is what each senior-to-family transition is really worth, per 100 homes:

The "downsizing dividend" pays for the program. The State's own STAR savings (~$2,058/yr per transition, forever) plus one-time transfer-tax receipts (~$3,300 per sale) exceed the cost of every incentive proposed in this deck — moving grants, deferral administration, exemption continuity. Albany finances senior mobility out of money it only collects because the senior moved.
About that missing number. How many RVC parcels actually hold §467 exemptions — and at which tiers — is knowable only from county assessment-roll data that is not currently published by school district. We deliberately publish no estimate. That is Ask #3: Nassau should publish exemption counts and exempted value by district before localities vote on the 65% tier or the 2027 income-cap expansion. Policy first requires arithmetic.

Honesty notes: enrollment decline ≠ measured building capacity (no public facilities study exists; we say "spare seats" only in that bounded sense). New students carry marginal costs that rise as spare capacity fills. All assumptions are labeled in the model at the link below.

None of this is novel — it's assembled from things that already work

PrecedentWhat it shows
Property-tax deferral
OR (since 1963) · MA Cl. 41A · WA · TX §33.06		Seniors stay cash-flow-safe; the taxing district is made whole with interest (5–8%) at sale; participation is voluntary; a lien — not a subsidy — does the work. Oregon's program is state-administered and self-financing.13
California Prop 19 (2020)55+ homeowners can carry their tax basis to any home statewide, up to 3 moves — passed 51% in the highest-property-tax-anxiety state in America, with the LAO scoring the package revenue-positive for schools. The lesson for NY is the politics (senior mobility wins at the ballot box), not the mechanism — NY taxes current value, so our version is benefit-continuity (S3309), not basis-porting.17
Minneapolis Fed (2024)"How higher property taxes increase home affordability" — recurring property taxes get capitalized into lower prices, reduce lock-in, raise turnover, and shift ownership toward younger families. NY's existing high property taxes mean the turnover dividend is unusually large here once the artificial locks are removed.18
NBER w25468 / J. Urban Econ. (2021)One hundred years of Georgia's age-based exemptions, quadruple-difference design: senior property-tax relief significantly increases older homeowners' retention in place. It's the rigorous version of what every Nassau assessor already knows — and why expansions like the new 65% tier need mobility counterweights.15
Levittown, 1976The endgame of doing nothing has a local address: when the first postwar suburb aged through its family cycle, it closed Pintail Lane and Cherrywood elementary schools. Those closures were two generations ago — the cycle is now Rockville Centre's to manage, with better tools.6
What we are not proposing. No reduction of any current senior's exemption or STAR benefit. No forced sales, no age-targeted assessments, no "empty-nester tax." Every mechanism here is opt-in for the senior and most are revenue-positive for the state. The message to RVC's 2,000+ senior-owner households: when you're ready, the move should cost you nothing extra.
The risk of inaction compounds. Each budget cycle under the cap starts ~$2–4M behind (health care + special-ed inflation vs ~2% levy + ~1% aid). The 2026-27 answer was 62 classroom positions. The 2027-28 question arrives with a higher senior exemption ceiling, fewer students, and an older electorate — every year of delay makes the carrots more expensive and the politics harder.
