# Consolidation & De-Consolidation as Natural Experiments

**Public-administration research brief — June 10, 2026**

**Throughline question:** When places actually merge (or split), do they capture the theoretical efficiency savings — or do *cost leveling-up*, *transition/adjustment costs*, and *political fracture* eat them?

**Headline answer:** Mostly the latter for **government** and **large-district** consolidations; the savings are real but **only for genuinely small units**. The peer-reviewed and case evidence converges on a sharp result: consolidation savings follow a **U-shaped curve** — large for merging tiny (≈300-pupil) districts, near-zero or negative once units pass ~1,500–2,000 pupils — and city-county government mergers show **no reliable reduction in total spending** at all. Meanwhile the dominant *equity* dynamic of the last 25 years runs the other way: **secession** of whiter, wealthier enclaves *out* of shared districts.

## 1. Memphis–Shelby County, TN (2013 merger → 2014 de-merger) — the marquee cautionary tale

**What happened.** In 2010–11 the Memphis City Schools (MCS) board voted to **surrender its charter**, dissolving MCS into the suburban-dominated Shelby County Schools (SCS) — creating, on July 1, 2013, the largest school-district merger in US history (the nation's ~14th-largest district at merger). Tennessee's legislature created a 21-member **Transition Planning Commission (TPC)**; the TPC hired the **Boston Consulting Group** and the Center on Reinventing Public Education to design the unified district. Just **one year later (Aug. 2014), six suburban municipalities seceded** — Arlington, Bartlett, Collierville, Germantown, Lakeland, Millington — to form their own districts, taking ~31,000 students.

**Why it was never really a "savings" play.** The merger was *forced* by governance/funding fear, not pitched on an efficiency projection. MCS surrendered its charter largely to stop the suburbs from incorporating a "special school district" that would have frozen the county tax base away from the city's poorer, majority-Black students. There was thus **no credible projected-savings headline** — instead the TPC plan opened with a **$68 million budget hole** for FY2012-13 (equal to the city's recent MCS contribution). Administrative-consolidation savings that *were* booked were modest: ~300 central-office layoffs (≈80% from the Memphis side), ~$15M from benefit cuts, ~$11.5M from custodial outsourcing, ~$3M from a unified IT system.

**Realized cost effect — the opposite of savings.** The merged SCS inherited an **OPEB (retiree health) liability exceeding $1 billion**, which Superintendent Dorsey Hopson called "a huge gorilla around our neck." When the suburbs seceded, they **left ~$300 million of that liability behind** while paying only ~$25M for the school buildings they kept. Result: SCS spent roughly **$570 of its $8,800 per-pupil allotment (~7%) on OPEB** — "every dollar we have to pay for [OPEB], we can't put in the classroom." Post-secession SCS faced **a $125 million deficit (2015)**, school closures under declining enrollment, and hundreds of teacher layoffs. Tennessee was **one of only six states with no legal mechanism to allocate "legacy costs"** when a district splits — so the cost fell entirely on the (poorer, Blacker) remnant.

**Equity / durability.** The merger **did not stick** — within ~14 months it fractured along the pre-existing racial/jurisdictional line. The six new suburban districts are whiter and wealthier; the remnant SCS is overwhelmingly low-income and Black. Academic verdict (Siegel-Hawley, Diem & Frankenberg, *AERJ* 2018) frames it as **resegregation via "local control."** As of 2025–26 the district is under a **state-backed takeover** — a sign the consolidation neither saved money nor stabilized governance.

> **Lesson:** Merging a big poor district into a big rich one does not capture scale economies (both were already past the efficient size); it just creates **one shared balance sheet that the wealthy side then exits** — leaving the stranded liabilities (here, $1B+ OPEB) on the students least able to bear them.

## 2. New York State's centralization wave (1910s–1960s) — the historical success, and where it stopped

**What drove it.** NY had **~10,000 school districts** at the start of the 20th century — mostly one-room "common school" districts (K–8). The **Central Rural Schools Act (1914, revised 1925)** plus the **Cole–Rice Law (Ch. 673, Laws of 1925)** introduced, for the first time, **state aid incentives** for reorganization: state money for **transportation, new buildings, and operations** if districts consolidated.

**Realized effect.** Highly effective on the rural/upstate margin: districts fell from **~10,000 (1925) to under 2,000 (1959)**, and to ~700 today. This is the textbook case where consolidation *did* deliver, precisely because the merging units were tiny one-room districts deep in the cost-saving zone of the curve.

**Why it STOPPED downstate / on Long Island.** The wave largely **spent itself before suburbanization filled in Nassau & Suffolk.** Long Island's districts formed/solidified *after* the consolidation push, as postwar suburban communities organized around **home rule** and local district identity. Today **Nassau + Suffolk still have ~125 separate districts / 656 schools**, and Long Island legislators and superintendents have repeatedly **killed state "regionalization" proposals** (most recently the 2023–24 NYSED regionalization plan) explicitly to "preserve local control." The political economy flipped: once districts were affluent and self-sufficient, the aid carrot couldn't overcome home-rule resistance.

**Duncombe & Yinger (Syracuse / Maxwell School) — the cost curve, with numbers.** Their NY rural-district studies (*"Does School District Consolidation Cut Costs?"*, *Education Finance & Policy* 2007) are the first *direct* estimates of consolidation cost effects, and they establish the **U-shaped / size-dependent** result:

- **Gross operating-cost savings from doubling enrollment:** large for small districts, small for big ones — roughly **~20%+ for a 300-pupil district falling toward single digits (~8%) by 1,500 pupils.**
- **Net savings after *adjustment/capital costs*** (the costs of actually merging — new buildings, transport, transition): fall sharply to **~31.5% for a 300-pupil merger and only ~14.4% for a 1,500-pupil merger** in the operating-cost paper's accounting; by the time **two 1,500-pupil districts** combine, capital-cost increases mean **"little, if any" net cost impact.**
- **Policy implication (their words):** state aid to cover consolidation's adjustment costs "appears warranted, **but only in relatively small districts**." For larger districts, the diseconomies and capital escalation can **wipe out or reverse** the savings.

A related Duncombe–Yinger–Zhang study (*Public Finance Review* 2016) found NY consolidations also **affected property values**, i.e., the effects are capitalized and not purely budgetary.

> **Lesson:** NY proves consolidation *can* save — but only in the **small-district regime**, and only when the state pays the transition cost. The same logic explains why it stalled downstate: those districts are already past the efficient size, so there's nothing to capture.

## 3. City-county GOVERNMENT consolidations — promised savings vs. the skeptical academic consensus

The scholarly bottom line (Carr & Feiock; Leland & Thurmaier; Taylor; Faulk & Grassmueck; an NRC review): **"there is general agreement that consolidation has not reduced costs and, in fact, may have even increased total local expenditures."** Savings, when found, show up in **specific functional line-items, not in total spending**, and often years later.

### Indianapolis "Unigov" (1970)
- **Method:** imposed by the **Indiana legislature with NO referendum** — the only major postwar consolidation done that way.
- **Savings verdict:** mixed-to-skeptical. In surveys of officials, **only ~54.6% believed economies of scale actually reduced costs.** Unigov is generally cited *against* the cost-savings thesis.
- **The catch that explains the "savings" — services were excluded:** **schools, welfare, fire districts, and some police were left at pre-Unigov borders.** So Unigov "consolidated everything but its schools," leaving **11 separate school districts**. Excluding the high-cost, redistributive services is exactly how a consolidation can look tidy without leveling spending up.
- **Equity / political fallout (the real story):** Unigov **diluted Black voting power**. Annexing the county added ~a quarter-million **white** suburbanites and few Black residents; the Black share of the city electorate fell back to **~18% (pre-1960 proportions)**. GOP chairman L. Keith Bulen called it "my greatest coup of all time… taking in 85,000 Republicans." A **federal judge later ruled the state "intentionally discriminated against the black pupils"** by extending city government to the county line while leaving IPS and ten ~all-white suburban districts intact (those suburban systems were **2.62% Black**, with 15 Black teachers out of 3,000+).

### Louisville–Jefferson County, KY (2003)
- **Projected:** a 1993 **Ernst & Young** study claimed merger could cost **~$9M up front but save ~$23M/year**, cutting ~400 positions across 19 duplicated service areas.
- **Realized:** the savings **did not appear.** When pressed, advocates couldn't substantiate the claims; an independent accounting review covering only **38% of the budget (excluding police & fire)** found **no cost savings and little real duplication.** Post-merger, inflation-adjusted Louisville Metro spending (~**$400–420M/yr**) was **similar to or greater than** the late-1990s separate totals. Univ. of Louisville's H.V. Savitch co-authored two studies concluding the merger **failed to deliver on its core promises.**

### Nashville–Davidson County, TN (1963)
- First major US city-county consolidation (voter-approved 1962, effective 1963). Sold on eliminating overlap (dual school systems, dual health departments) and the free-rider problem of county residents using city services untaxed.
- **Savings verdict:** **no rigorous study isolates net cost savings**; like the others it is generally read as a **governance/service-coordination success, not a documented money-saver.** Consistent with the literature, gains were in service *extension and coordination*, not reduced total spending.

> **Lesson:** City-county mergers reliably **expand and coordinate** services and can boost economic-development capacity — but the **"we'll save money" pitch is the part the research does not support.** And the cleanest-looking ones (Indianapolis) achieved tidiness partly by **excluding the redistributive services and diluting minority political power.**

## 4. School-district SECESSION — the reverse pressure (EdBuild, *Fractured*)

**The trend.** EdBuild's *Fractured* (2017; updated 2019) documents the **opposite** of consolidation: communities **splitting OFF** to form their own districts. Since 2000:
- **2017 report:** **101 attempts, 63 successful** secessions (2000–2016).
- **2019 update:** **~73 successful secessions out of ~120 attempts** since 2000, with ~10 in the prior two years and ~17 ongoing.

**Who leaves and what it does.** A **clear majority of seceding districts are whiter and wealthier** than the districts they leave: higher median household income, higher property values, lower student poverty, **lower shares of nonwhite students**, and higher local tax revenue per district. Because **~half of US K-12 funding is local (property tax)**, secession lets the affluent side **wall off its tax base for "hyperlocal" schools**, stranding a needier, more nonwhite population with fewer resources. Example: **Monroe Local (OH)**, seceded from Middletown in 2000 — by 2015 its median property value (~$159,200) was **>70% higher** than Middletown's.

**Policy enabling it.** **30 states** have codified secession processes; **most include no consideration for the students left behind**, and since 2017 **two states made secession even easier.** EdBuild calls it **"a new, twenty-first-century approach to segregation."** Secession also **duplicates bureaucracies** (the opposite of the efficiency rationale), so it tends to *raise* total system cost while worsening equity.

> **Lesson:** The live political force today is not consolidation but **fracture** — and it is **regressive by construction**: it concentrates wealth and lowers cost *for the secessionists* while raising it for everyone else.

Sources: [EdBuild, *Fractured* (full report PDF)](https://edbuild.org/content/fractured/fractured-full-report.pdf) · [The 74 — secession & "state-sanctioned" segregation](https://www.the74million.org/article/when-communities-secede-from-school-districts-inequity-segregation-follow-but-30-states-let-it-happen-anyway/) · [EdWeek — wealthy enclaves splitting](https://www.edweek.org/leadership/wealthier-enclaves-breaking-away-from-school-districts/2019/04) · [Hechinger Report](https://hechingerreport.org/school-district-secessions-gather-speed-a-new-report-shows/)

## 5. NY school mergers post-2000 — small upstate deals & the aid incentive

**The incentive.** NY's current carrot: a merging pair receives **+40% extra "Reorganization Incentive Operating Aid" for the first 5 years**, then it phases down ~4 points/year and **runs out in year 15** (plus enhanced building aid). This is a substantial sweetener — e.g., the proposed **Johnsburg–Minerva** merger would have brought a **~$11 million aid increase.**

**The realized record — incentives often aren't enough; voters say no.** Despite the money, **referendums frequently fail** on community-identity grounds (sports teams, school closures, travel time): **Johnsburg–Minerva was voted down in fall 2024.** Where small upstate districts *do* merge, results track Duncombe–Yinger: **genuine operating savings when the units are tiny**, partly offset by transition/capital costs and busing — so the state aid is effectively *buying* the savings rather than the savings being free. The Rockefeller Institute and NYSED continue to push **"regionalization"** (sharing services short of full merger) as the more politically feasible route to the same scale economies.

> **Lesson:** Even with a 40% aid bribe and clear small-district savings, **local identity routinely vetoes mergers** — confirming that the binding constraint on capturing consolidation savings is **political, not financial.**

Sources: [NYSUT Fact Sheet #25-03, mergers/consolidations](https://www.nysut.org/-/media/files/nysut/resources/2025/2025_01_28_factsheet_25_3_school_district_mergers_consolidations.pdf) · [RBT CPAs — NY increased merger aid](https://www.rbtcpas.com/thought-leadership-articles/education/to-merge-or-not-to-merge-new-york-state-offering-increased-aid-to-consolidating-school-districts/) · [Rockefeller Institute — regionalization](https://rockinst.org/blog/school-district-regionalization-more-affordable-services-greater-opportunities-for-students/) · [NYSED Guide to Reorganization](https://www.p12.nysed.gov/mgtserv/sch_dist_org/GuideToReorganizationOfSchoolDistricts.htm)

## Synthesis — answering the throughline

| Case | Projected | Realized | Stuck? | Equity verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Memphis–Shelby (2013→14) | No real savings projection; $68M budget hole | $1B+ OPEB liability stranded on remnant; $125M deficit, closures, layoffs | **No** — fractured in ~14 mo; state takeover by 2026 | Resegregation; suburbs exit, poor/Black students stranded |
| NY centralization (1914–1959) | Aid-driven scale savings | **Real:** ~10,000 → <2,000 districts; large savings on tiny units | **Yes** upstate; **stalled** downstate | Pro-equity within the rural regime; LI home rule blocked it |
| Indianapolis Unigov (1970) | Efficiency/econ-dev | No reliable total-cost savings (~55% of officials skeptical) | Yes structurally | **Diluted Black vote**; schools/welfare excluded; judge found intentional discrimination |
| Louisville (2003) | E&Y: save ~$23M/yr, cut 400 jobs | **No savings**; spending ≥ pre-merger | Yes structurally | Promises unmet; benefits oversold |
| Nashville (1963) | Eliminate overlap/save | Service coordination ✓; **no documented net savings** | Yes | Mixed |
| Secession / *Fractured* (2000–) | "local control," efficiency | **Duplicated bureaucracy → higher system cost**; wealth walled off | Trend accelerating, 30 states | **Regressive**; "21st-century segregation" |
| NY mergers post-2000 | +40% aid, 5 yr | Small-district savings real but **bought** by aid; **referendums often fail** | Often **no** (voter veto) | Mostly neutral |

**The pattern.** Theoretical scale economies are **captured only in the small-unit regime** (Duncombe–Yinger: ~20%+ net for ~300-pupil mergers, vanishing by ~1,500 pupils), and **only when the state pays the transition cost**. For **large** school mergers and for **city-county government** mergers, the savings are eaten by (a) **cost leveling-up** (the cheaper unit's wages/benefits rise to the richer unit's), (b) **transition/adjustment & capital costs**, and (c) **political fracture** — the wealthy side either blocks the merger (Long Island), exits afterward (Memphis suburbs), or carves out the redistributive services up front (Indianapolis schools/welfare). And the strongest current force, **secession**, runs the efficiency logic in reverse for an explicitly regressive payoff.
