# Does school-district consolidation actually cut costs? A hard-numbers comparison across NYC- and DC-metro suburbs

**Research date:** June 10, 2026
**Question:** Do county-wide *consolidated* school districts (Montgomery MD, Fairfax VA) spend materially **less per pupil on administration** than *fragmented* counties like Nassau NY (56 districts) and Bergen NJ (~70)? Does *total* per-pupil spending track fragmentation at all, or do labor costs / cost-of-living swamp it? Is there an outcome difference?

**One-line answer:** Consolidation produces a **large, real administration savings** (~$1,000–$1,900 per pupil, roughly 2–3x) but a **small or zero total-spending savings** — because cost-of-living and teacher labor dominate the budget, and the academic literature says scale economies are exhausted by ~2,000–4,000 students (every district below is already past that). Outcomes are statistically indistinguishable across the gradient.

## THE CRUX FINDING: administration per pupil

Using the **identical NCES "Administration" function** across all districts (general + school administration + central support):

| | Admin $/pupil | vs Montgomery |
|---|---|---|
| Montgomery MD (1 district, 161K students) | **$968** | — |
| Broward FL (1 district, 244K) | $1,263 | +30% |
| Fairfax VA (1 district, 179K) | $1,392 | +44% |
| Ridgewood (Bergen NJ, 5,480) | $2,082 | **+115%** |
| Port Washington (Nassau, 5,348) | $2,677 | **+177%** |
| Garden City (Nassau, 4,064) | $2,840 | **+193%** |
| Uniondale (Nassau, 6,189) | $2,873 | **+197%** |

**The clearest single statistic: Montgomery County MD spends $968/pupil on administration vs. roughly $2,800/pupil in fragmented Nassau districts — Nassau spends about 2.9x (≈$1,900/pupil) more on administration.** Against Fairfax ($1,392) the gap narrows to ~2x but is still ~$1,300–$1,500/pupil. This is the duplication-of-overhead effect: Nassau runs **56 superintendents, 56 boards, 56 back-offices** for 195K students; Montgomery runs **one** superintendent and central office for 161K, with internal *regions* (Fairfax: 5–6 regions, each an assistant superintendent + executive principals; Montgomery: community/region structure) substituting for independent district overhead.

At ~$1,900/pupil x 194,855 Nassau students, the *gross* admin-overhead gap implies **~$370M/year** that fragmented Nassau spends on administration above the Montgomery per-pupil rate. (This is a ceiling, not a clean "savings available," because a consolidated Nassau would still need regional administrators and its NY-metro labor rates would exceed Maryland's — see caveats.)

## BUT: total per-pupil does NOT cleanly track fragmentation

- Nassau (56 districts): **~$32,700/pupil**; individual wealthy districts $28K–$36K.
- Westchester (~40 districts): **$32,400/pupil**.
- Montgomery (1 district): **$19,456/pupil**.
- Fairfax (1 district): **$18,975/pupil**.
- Broward (1 district): **$12,121/pupil**.

The total gap (Nassau ~$32.7K vs Montgomery ~$19.5K = ~$13,000/pupil) is **~7x larger than the admin gap (~$1,900)**. Administration is only ~5–10% of any of these budgets. The bulk of the Nassau–Montgomery total difference is **NOT administrative duplication** — it is:

1. **Teacher labor / cost-of-living.** Long Island salaries, pensions, and benefits are far above suburban Maryland/Virginia. Instruction $/pupil: Garden City **$19,181**, Uniondale **$24,909**, Port Washington **$20,665** vs Montgomery **$13,211**, Fairfax **$11,588**. The instructional gap alone (~$6K–$11K/pupil) dwarfs the admin gap.
2. **NY vs MD/VA funding/spending culture** (NY spends the most per pupil of any state).

**Verdict on "does total track fragmentation": weak-to-none.** Westchester (40 districts) and Nassau (56) spend nearly the same ($32.4K vs $32.7K) despite different fragmentation; Broward (1 district) spends $12K while Fairfax (1 district) spends $19K — a 1.6x spread *among consolidated counties*, driven entirely by region/labor cost, not structure. Cost-of-living and labor swamp fragmentation in the total.

## OUTCOMES: no penalty for fragmentation

| County | Grad rate | Structure |
|---|---|---|
| Fairfax VA | **93.8%** (2023) | 1 district |
| Nassau NY | **94%** (2023) | 56 districts |
| Montgomery MD | **89.6%** (2023) | 1 district |
| Bergen NJ | ~91% (NJ statewide proxy) | ~70 districts |

Fragmented Nassau (94%) edges out both consolidated DC-metro giants on graduation rate; Montgomery (the most-consolidated, lowest-admin case) is *lowest* of the four. There is **no evidence that consolidation buys better outcomes**, nor that fragmentation buys them. (Caveat: grad rates compress at the top among affluent suburbs; demographic mix differs — Montgomery and Broward serve higher-poverty populations than Nassau's wealthiest districts. NAEP district-level [TUDA] data would be cleaner but Montgomery/Fairfax/Nassau are not TUDA participants, so state grad rate is the best uniform proxy.)

## WHY THE ADMIN SAVINGS IS REAL BUT THE TOTAL SAVINGS IS A MIRAGE: the academic consensus

This matches the peer-reviewed literature almost exactly:

- **Duncombe & Yinger (Syracuse), 12 actual NY consolidations:** doubling a **300-student** district yields ~22.8% net savings; doubling a **1,500-student** district yields only ~3.2%; **little or no savings for mergers of districts already above 1,500 students.** Operating-cost economies are essentially exhausted by **2,000–4,000 pupils.**
- **Every fragmented district in this comparison is already 4,000–6,200 students** — past the efficient frontier. So merging Nassau's *instructional* spending would save little; the literature predicts the win is concentrated in **administrative overhead** (eliminating redundant superintendents/boards), exactly the line where the data shows the 2–3x gap.
- **Diseconomies of scale above ~15,000 students** (extra central-management layers, transportation, security). Broward (244K) and a hypothetical 195K-student "Nassau Unified" would sit deep in diseconomy territory — which is *why* a consolidated Nassau would still need regional administration and could not realistically hit Montgomery's $968 floor.
- Duncombe & Yinger explicitly flag that **consolidation can sometimes raise administrative costs** if salary harmonization levels everyone up to the highest pre-merger scale — a real risk in high-wage Long Island.

**Net:** the honest read is that consolidation's savings are **specific and bounded** — they live in the general-administration line (fewer superintendents/boards), not in the 90% of the budget that is teachers and buildings. For Nassau, the realistically capturable prize is the admin overhead, on the order of **$1,000–$1,900/pupil** ($200M–$370M/yr gross), *minus* the regional-administration and labor-harmonization costs a 195K-student unified district would incur — likely netting to a meaningful but far smaller figure than the gross gap implies. This is precisely why NY's actual Nassau initiatives target **shared back-office services through BOCES/county** (purchasing, IT, audit, legal — an identified ~$5M starter) rather than full merger: it captures the admin economy without the diseconomy and political cost of dissolving 56 districts.

## GOVERNANCE: how the one-district counties preserve local input

- **Fairfax VA:** Subdivided into **5–6 Regions**, each led by an **assistant superintendent + executive principals**, organized around high-school "pyramids" (a high school and its feeder schools). Regions hire/evaluate principals and act as school-community liaison — local responsiveness without independent districts.
- **Montgomery MD:** Single elected Board of Education + one superintendent, with internal community/regional offices over school clusters; magnet/regional programs provide differentiation.
- **Broward FL:** County-wide by Florida statute (all FL districts are county-wide); single board, regional cadre.
- **Nassau NY (contrast):** 56 independent elected boards + 56 superintendents, networked through **Nassau BOCES** (a regional cooperative providing shared special-ed, technical, and back-office services) and the Nassau County Council of School Superintendents. The "central high school district" structure (Bellmore-Merrick, Sewanhaka, Valley Stream) is itself a *partial* consolidation: K-8 component districts feed a shared CHSD — evidence Nassau already uses limited regionalization at the secondary level.

## CAVEATS / DATA-QUALITY FLAGS

1. **County rollup vs named districts.** NCES publishes F-33 admin by *district*, not by *multi-district county*. For Nassau/Bergen/Westchester the admin $/pupil is represented by named districts spanning the wealth spectrum (VERIFIED at district level); a single county-weighted admin average is an ESTIMATE. The 3 named Nassau districts ($2,677–$2,873) are tightly clustered, so the ~$2,800 figure is robust, but a true enrollment-weighted Nassau admin average could shift modestly.
2. **NY ESSA categories ≠ F-33 functions.** NYSED's transparency portal reports per-pupil by ESSA central/school-level buckets, which are *not* directly comparable to NCES F-33 "general administration / school administration." All cross-state admin comparisons here use the **NCES district-detail "Administration" line**, which IS uniform.
3. **Year alignment.** Core finance figures are FY2022-23 (NCES). Some Nassau/Westchester totals cited are 2022 (NYSED/Westchester Index) and Bergen budgetary figures are 2021-22 — within one year; directionally safe but not identical vintages.
4. **Grad-rate compression & demographics.** Affluent suburbs cluster at 89–94%; Montgomery and Broward serve higher-poverty populations, so their slightly lower rates are not evidence of a consolidation penalty. Treat the outcome row as "no large difference," not a precise ranking.
5. **Enrollment basis.** Per-pupil uses F-33 fall enrollment; small differences vs NYSED BEDS-day counts exist (Nassau 194,855 is NYSED 2021-22 K-12).
