# Does a Single Mega-Superintendent Buy Real Political Leverage?

**Hypothesis tested:** Running a huge number of students under ONE superintendent/chancellor buys real political leverage with state and federal government — education gets treated as a peer at the governing table — versus being fragmented across many small districts.

**Date:** June 10, 2026
**Method:** Web search/fetch against academic sources (Wong & Shen, Henig, Kirst-adjacent CAP work), news of record (Chalkbeat, City & State, LA-ist, Las Vegas Review-Journal, Honolulu Civil Beat), state budget/finance docs (NYS Budget, FL DOE FEFP, CA LAO), and comptroller reports.

---

## VERDICT (one line)

**Scale buys a SEAT and a NAME — a single accountable interlocutor the governor/mayor must deal with — but the evidence does NOT show scale buys a better DEAL: big districts more often *lose* on per-pupil formula treatment, and the federal money that matters tracks poverty, not structure. Scale converts to visibility and a standing negotiation, not to a structural funding premium.**

---

## Q1. Does the single mega-leader function as a peer/interlocutor with mayor, governor, legislature? — YES, clearly.

This is the strongest leg of the hypothesis. A mega-district with one named leader creates a **single accountable face** that general-purpose government must transact with, and it generates **standalone, recurring fights in the capital** that fragmented districts never command.

**The theory (Wong; Henig).** Kenneth Wong's core claim for mayoral control is precisely "a single office responsible for the performance of the city's public schools" — integrating school accountability into the general electoral process so there is one office to hold accountable ([CAP, Mayoral Governance and Student Achievement](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/mayoral-governance-and-student-achievement/); [Wong & Shen, *The Education Mayor*](https://www.hfsbooks.com/books/the-education-mayor-wong/)). Jeffrey Henig's *The End of Exceptionalism* (Harvard Ed Press, 2013) frames the deeper mechanism: education historically used **single-purpose governance** (independent school boards) that made it "exceptional" and insulated — but that insulation also kept it *off* the main table. The shift to **general-purpose governance** (mayors, governors, legislatures) is what gives "education clout in the broader policy conversation," since education otherwise "ranks outside top national priorities" ([Fordham summary of Henig](https://fordhaminstitute.org/ohio/commentary/end-exceptionalism-american-education-changing-politics-school-reform)). So the academic literature *supports* the leverage-from-concentration claim — with an explicit warning (see Q4) that integration also dilutes education's distinct voice.

**Concrete evidence the mega-leader negotiates state law directly:**

- **NYC mayoral control is a standalone Albany fight, on a recurring clock.** Because NYC's ~900K–1M-student system runs under mayoral control with a sunset, the city's governance *itself* is renegotiated in the capital every few years. In spring 2026, **Chancellor Kamar Samuels personally traveled to Albany** to lobby legislators for a four-year extension — a named official directly negotiating state law ([NY1, Mar 2026](https://ny1.com/nyc/all-boroughs/news/2026/03/24/doe-chancellor-albany-smaller-class-size-mayoral-control); [Spectrum, Mar 2026](https://spectrumlocalnews.com/nys/buffalo/news/2026/03/24/doe-chancellor-albany-smaller-class-size-mayoral-control)). No 2,000-student district ever has its existence debated on the floor of the legislature.

- **The renewal is a live bargaining chip.** The 2026 budget granted only a **2-year extension (through June 2028)**, not the 4 years sought, explicitly *because* legislators wanted leverage over the city's class-size compliance — State Sen. John Liu said the length "will always hinge upon the actions of the administration… in the eyes of [the] legislature" ([Chalkbeat, May 20 2026](https://www.chalkbeat.org/newyork/2026/05/20/ny-budget-deal-mayoral-control-school-funding-foundation-aid-mamdani-2026/); [NY1, Mar 23 2026](https://ny1.com/nyc/all-boroughs/news/2026/03/23/mamdani-unlikely-to-get-four-years-of-mayoral-control--top-state-lawmakers-say)). The single-leader structure is literally *the unit of negotiation*.

- **NYC has a dedicated, named line in the state budget conversation.** Foundation Aid to NYC is negotiated as its own quantity — the 2026 deal raised NYC Foundation Aid by ~**$538M (≈5%)** to nearly **$11B**, plus **$143M** targeted to homeless/foster/ELL students ([Chalkbeat, May 20 2026](https://www.chalkbeat.org/newyork/2026/05/20/ny-budget-deal-mayoral-control-school-funding-foundation-aid-mamdani-2026/)). The state budget's school-aid run is literally organized around named "Big Five" districts.

- **The mega-district can sue the State as a peer and win statewide policy.** *Campaign for Fiscal Equity v. State of New York* (filed 1993; Court of Appeals rulings 1995, 2003) was brought on behalf of NYC's ~1M students and forced a court-ordered **$5.5B statewide Foundation Aid increase** and the creation of the modern Foundation Aid formula ([Wikipedia: CFE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campaign_for_Fiscal_Equity); [NYCLU](https://www.nyclu.org/court-cases/campaign-fiscal-equity-v-state-new-york-challenging-states-failure-provide-nyc-children-sound); [TC Center for Educational Equity](https://cee.tc.columbia.edu/ny-school-funding-project/what-is-the-cfe-campaign-for-fiscal-equity-lawsuit/what-is-the-cfe-campaign-for-fiscal-equity-lawsuit.php)). The concentrated plaintiff *moved the whole state formula*.

- **LAUSD's scale draws federal and state attention.** Board members control an **~$18.8B budget**; the superintendency is consequential enough that the FBI searched Supt. Alberto Carvalho's home/office and the board placed him on leave ([LA-ist](https://laist.com/news/lausd-los-angeles-school-board-schmerelson-koziatek-castellanos-ortiz-franklin-bd3-bd7)). Scale = a name big enough to be a national story.

**Conclusion Q1:** TRUE. The single mega-leader is a genuine peer/interlocutor: a named official who lobbies the capitol in person, whose governance is itself a recurring legislative bill, who litigates the State directly, and who occupies a dedicated line in budget talks. Fragmented small districts cannot do any of this individually — they must aggregate through associations, diluting the single accountable name.

## Q2. Does scale translate to MORE resources / better per-pupil formula treatment? — NO. Often the OPPOSITE.

This is where the hypothesis **fails**. The evidence runs against "size = better deal" and toward "size = political target / structurally disadvantaged on formulas."

**Big cities chronically argue they send more than they get back, and lose share over time.**

- CFE's founding grievance: NYC had **~38% of the state's students** (and a disproportionate share of poverty/special-needs students) but received only **~36% of state aid** ([Wikipedia: CFE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campaign_for_Fiscal_Equity)).

- The NYS "shares agreement" is **not a needs formula — it's a geo-political gentlemen's agreement**: NYC's share was frozen near **38.86%** (set by the Assembly in the mid-1980s as the city lost population), and a **Long Island share of 12.96%** was bolted on in 1989 when an Oyster Bay Republican (Marino) became Senate Majority Leader. Each year's aid increase is carved by these fixed percentages "regardless of need" ([City & State, "How the school aid formula became unrecognizable"](https://cityandstateny.com/articles/policy/education/how-school-aid-formula-became-unrecognizable.html)). Scale didn't win NYC a *premium*; it won a *frozen quota* that advocates (AQE's Billy Easton) call a source of inequity.

- Even recent "tweaks" cut against the giant: 2025 formula changes left NYC schools roughly **$314M short** of what the prior formula would have delivered ([Chalkbeat, May 2025](https://www.chalkbeat.org/newyork/2025/05/08/foundation-aid-formula-kathy-hochul-state-budget-nyc-schools/)).

- **The local burden has shifted onto the city.** Where the City and State once contributed *roughly equally* to NYC schools, the **City's share now far exceeds the State's** ([City & State opinion, Nov 2025](https://www.cityandstateny.com/opinion/2025/11/opinion-new-york-must-stop-shortchanging-schools-yesterdays-data/409693/)). Scale did not protect the city from being asked to self-fund.

**Direct counter-evidence: SMALL districts get MORE per pupil, and very-large districts under-perform mid-size on formula efficiency.** California's Legislative Analyst found districts with **<100 students average >$18,000/pupil — more than twice** what districts of ≥1,000 students receive, and explicitly found **no persuasive evidence** that consolidating into larger units saves money or improves outcomes; it even notes **mid-size districts outperform "exceptionally large" districts** ([CA LAO, 2011](https://lao.ca.gov/reports/2011/edu/district_consolidation/district_consolidation_050211.aspx)). The Governing/consolidation literature concurs that states *reward* smallness — very small districts get "more than twice as much funding per pupil" ([Governing](https://www.governing.com/topics/education/gov-fragmented-school-districts-consolidation.html)). On the pure formula math, **fragmentation often pays better per child than concentration.**

**Federal money tracks poverty, not the single-leader structure.** ESSER (~$189.5B) flowed via the **Title I-A formula** — number and concentration of low-income students — not district governance ([New America](https://www.newamerica.org/insights/k-12-federal-covid-relief-what-can-we-learn-from-doing-school-funding-differently/size-more-money-than-ever-before/); [Pandemic Oversight](https://www.pandemicoversight.gov/data-interactive-tools/data-stories/states-received-1895-billion-relief-schools-heres-breakdown)). NYC's **$7.66B** and the NY Big Five's **$8.6B** ([NYS Comptroller DiNapoli](https://www.osc.ny.gov/press/releases/2022/11/dinapoli-report-finds-big-five-school-districts-use-pandemic-aid-reflects-different-priorities)) reflect that NYC has enormous *concentrated poverty enrollment* — a county-wide consolidation of equally poor kids would have drawn the same dollars. The big check follows poor children, not the chancellor's title.

**Florida shows scale can even *disqualify* you from supplements.** Miami-Dade (~330K, largest in FL) and Broward (~250K) are too big to qualify for the FEFP **sparsity supplement** (cutoff: >20,000 FTE), which steers extra money to *small* rural districts ([FL DOE FEFP statistical report](https://www.fldoe.org/core/fileparse.php/7507/urlt/Fefpdist.pdf); [OPPAGA FEFP summary](https://oppaga.fl.gov/ProgramSummary/BackPageDetail?programNumber=2002&backPageNumber=01)). (Miami-Dade/Broward do get a niche carve-out — lottery/slot-machine proceeds in the Educational Enhancement Trust Fund — but that's a revenue quirk, not a per-pupil formula premium.)

**Conclusion Q2:** The hypothesis is FALSE here. Scale buys a *guaranteed quota and a loud grievance*, not a structural per-pupil premium. Big-city districts perennially run "we subsidize the rest of the state" fights and frequently *lose share*; small districts are often *rewarded* on per-pupil formulas; federal dollars track poverty concentration regardless of governance. Size = political target as much as political beneficiary.

## Q3. Does concentrated governance improve outcomes/accountability — or just visibility? — MOSTLY VISIBILITY + ACCOUNTABILITY; OUTCOME GAINS ARE REAL BUT MODEST, UNEVEN, AND FADE. (Balanced.)

**The "yes" case (Wong & Shen).** *The Education Mayor* (empirical panel 1999–2003 + case studies, ~100 districts/40 states) found **bottom-quartile schools in mayor-controlled districts made real gains** — e.g., Baltimore 3rd-grade reading 5.6%→32.7% proficient; Chicago 5th-grade math 10.4%→27.5% in the lowest performers ([Ed Week summary](https://www.edweek.org/ew/articles/2009/10/14/07wallace-wong.h29.html)). CAP's review credits NYC with "significant positive effects," ~1–3% annual gains, and lists NYC/Chicago/Boston/Philadelphia/Baltimore among districts narrowing gaps ([CAP](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/mayoral-governance-and-student-achievement/)).

**The "but" case — and it's substantial:**
- Mayoral control is **"less effective at narrowing the gap between top and bottom quartiles"** ([Ed Week / Wong & Shen](https://www.edweek.org/ew/articles/2009/10/14/07wallace-wong.h29.html)).
- Gains **taper**: Boston and Chicago showed strong early improvement then "a relative tapering of performance" ([CAP](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/mayoral-governance-and-student-achievement/)).
- Wong & Shen's own 2005 finances/staffing study of the 100 largest districts found **mayoral takeovers did NOT improve financial stability** ([CAP](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/mayoral-governance-and-student-achievement/)).
- The accountability claim is framed by Wong as *structural* ("a single office responsible") — i.e., the durable product is **clear responsibility and coherence**, not guaranteed test scores.

**The democratic-legitimacy backlash is now reversing the trend.** Chicago is **ending ~30 years of mayoral control**, moving to a 21-member **hybrid (10 elected / 11 appointed) board in Jan 2025, fully elected by 2027** ([WTTW, Dec 2024](https://news.wttw.com/2024/12/17/mayor-brandon-johnson-picks-10-new-school-board-members-mayoral-control-cps-ends); [City of Chicago](https://www.chicago.gov/city/en/depts/mayor/press_room/press_releases/2025/february/hybrid-school-board.html)). Even NYC's 2026 mayor (Mamdani) *reversed course* on mayoral control under political pressure before re-embracing it ([Chalkbeat, Dec 2025](https://www.chalkbeat.org/newyork/2025/12/31/zohran-mamdani-taps-kamar-samuels-as-chancellor-and-reverses-mayoral-control-stance/)). If concentration's outcome payoff were large and durable, the political momentum wouldn't be toward dispersing it.

**Conclusion Q3:** Concentrated governance most reliably delivers **a single accountable name + administrative coherence + visibility**. Outcome gains exist (especially for the lowest performers) but are **modest, uneven, gap-widening-resistant, and tend to fade** — and they don't extend to fiscal health. Visibility and accountability, more than guaranteed results.

## Q4. The pathologies of scale — what the big voice COSTS in responsiveness.

The single big voice carries real costs, documented across every county-wide/statewide case:

- **Procurement disasters at scale.** LAUSD's **~$1.3B iPad program** collapsed amid a no-RFP push by the vendor, a Pearson foundation-funded poolside junket with free iPads, an FBI probe, and a **$6.45M Pearson settlement** ([NPR](https://www.npr.org/sections/ed/2014/08/27/343549939/the-l-a-school-ipad-scandal-what-you-need-to-know); [Ed Week MarketBrief](https://marketbrief.edweek.org/regulation-policy/feds-drop-investigation-into-los-angeles-district-over-1-billion-ipad-purchase/2017/02)). Big single buyers create big single failures.

- **Scandal surface grows with the org.** Clark County (~300K) saw a **$10M construction-management contract steered to a firm owned by the bid-overseer's wife** (~$1.5M payout), plus county stonewalling of investigation records ([Las Vegas Review-Journal](https://www.reviewjournal.com/investigations/county-investigation-manager-heavily-involved-in-projects-involving-wifes-firm-3832720/)). A class-action also alleges **"systematic failure" in special education** ([MSN/RJ](https://www.msn.com/en-us/news/us/systematic-failure-class-action-lawsuit-alleges-special-education-problems-in-clark-county-school-district/ar-AA1rlhxk)).

- **Board/union/charter capture turns governance into an arms race.** Single-purpose board races become the cheapest lever to control a multi-billion-dollar system: LAUSD's 2017 races hit **~$15M (then a national record)**, since eclipsed by 2026 as the most expensive ever, with the Charter Schools Association out-spending the union ~6:1 in some seats ([The 74](https://www.the74million.org/article/los-angeles-school-board-race-reshape-second-biggest-district/); [LA-ist](https://laist.com/news/lausd-los-angeles-school-board-schmerelson-koziatek-castellanos-ortiz-franklin-bd3-bd7)). This is exactly the **single-purpose "capture by intense interests"** failure Henig warns about ([Fordham/Henig](https://fordhaminstitute.org/ohio/commentary/end-exceptionalism-american-education-changing-politics-school-reform)).

- **Distance from community → active deconsolidation pressure.** Clark County residents called the 15-municipality district a "mammoth bureaucracy" where "parents really have no say"; Nevada's response was **AB469 (2017)**, breaking schools into ~200+ semi-autonomous **"local precincts"** with site-based budgeting — *re-fragmenting governance inside the giant* precisely to restore responsiveness ([ECS, Deconsolidation: Clark County](https://www.ecs.org/deconsolidation-challenges-and-considerations-a-lesson-from-clark-county-nevada/); [Nevada Independent](https://thenevadaindependent.com/article/eight-years-later-why-are-lombardo-and-lawmakers-trying-to-scale-back-clark-county-schools-reorganization)).

- **Hawaii (the structural extreme: entire STATE = one district) shows the accountability paradox most starkly.** A single statewide system buys uniform funding and curriculum and avoids Oahu-vs-neighbor-island disparities — but Gov. Cayetano concluded it "distanced Hawaii's people from their schools" and made the system "less able to address the State's educational needs," and advocates note you "can't hold principals accountable… they're only enforcing decisions made somewhere else." Breaking it up has been debated since **1977** and recurs every few years ([Honolulu Civil Beat: decades-old debate](https://www.civilbeat.org/2023/02/the-decades-old-debate-over-breaking-up-hawaiis-statewide-school-system/); [Civil Beat: should be decentralized](https://www.civilbeat.org/2022/11/hawaiis-public-school-system-should-be-decentralized/)). The maximal single voice yields **maximal distance and diffused accountability.**

- **Henig's structural warning.** Concentration/integration "promises influence but risks dilution"; "with so many cooks in the kitchen, there may be little hope for policy coherence" ([Fordham/Henig](https://fordhaminstitute.org/ohio/commentary/end-exceptionalism-american-education-changing-politics-school-reform)). The same move that wins a seat at the general-government table can submerge education's distinct agenda beneath competing mayoral/gubernatorial priorities.

**Conclusion Q4:** The single big voice costs responsiveness, invites mega-scale procurement failure and a larger scandal/capture surface, and provokes recurring deconsolidation movements — to the point that the largest systems (Clark County, Hawaii) actively re-fragment *internally* to claw back the community proximity that scale destroys.

---

## BOTTOM LINE

**Is "one big superintendent = a real seat at the table that fragmented districts lack" TRUE?**
**Partly — and precisely on the *seat*, not the *winnings*.**

1. **Seat at the table: TRUE.** A single mega-leader is a genuine peer/interlocutor — a named official who lobbies the legislature in person, whose governance is itself a recurring bill (NYC mayoral-control renewals), who can litigate the State and move the entire statewide formula (CFE), and who holds a dedicated line in budget negotiations. Fragmented small districts can only achieve this by aggregating into associations, which forfeits the single accountable name. This is real and unique to scale.

2. **How much does the seat WIN? Surprisingly little in pure resource terms — and sometimes it loses.** Big-city districts chronically argue "we send more than we get back" (NYC: 38% of students, 36% of aid), get frozen into political *quotas* rather than needs premiums (the 38.86% NYC / 12.96% LI "shares" deal), watch the local burden shift onto the city, and can lose formula share year to year ($314M NYC shortfall, 2025). **Small districts often get MORE per pupil** (CA LAO: <100 students ≈ $18K/pupil, 2× the rate of ≥1,000-student districts), federal dollars track **poverty, not structure** (ESSER via Title I), and very-large size can even *disqualify* a district from supplements (FL sparsity) and underperform mid-size on outcomes. The seat is real; the structural payout is not.

3. **Outcomes/accountability: modest and fading, mostly visibility.** Mayoral control delivers a single accountable name and administrative coherence and some bottom-quartile gains, but the gains are uneven, don't close top-bottom gaps, taper over time, don't fix finances — and the political tide (Chicago→elected board; Mamdani's wobble) is moving *away* from concentration.

4. **The seat has a price.** Scale brings mega-procurement failure (LAUSD iPads), a larger scandal/capture surface (Clark County; record-shattering LA board-race spending), and such distance from community that the biggest systems re-fragment internally (Nevada's "precincts") or debate breakup for half a century (Hawaii).

**Net:** Scale buys **voice and a standing negotiation** — a real, durable seat at the governing table that fragmentation cannot replicate — but it largely buys **visibility and accountability rather than a better financial deal or guaranteed results**. The mega-superintendent gets to *argue* as a peer; the evidence does not show they consistently *win* more per child for it, and on the cleanest formula comparisons they often win less. **A seat at the table — not a bigger slice off it.**
