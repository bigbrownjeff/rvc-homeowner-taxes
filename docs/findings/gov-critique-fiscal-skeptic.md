# Fiscal-Skeptic Critique of the Lead Ranking

**Lens:** Skeptical public-finance economist. Test: which levers reduce a *recurring, homeowner-visible* dollar on the **named RVC homeowner's** bill — not the county budget, not "a homeowner somewhere," not a one-time capital event.

---

## The kill shot

**Lever #1, the entire dollar foundation of the memo, does not touch the named RVC homeowner's bill — and the strategist's own report proves it.** The Sec. 2 stack table shows Rockville Centre provides its **own village sanitation** (and its own PD, water, and municipal electric); RVC homeowners are *not* in a Town of Hempstead commissioner garbage district. So the audited ~$18M/yr Hempstead sanitation recovery and the "3x cost gap" accrue to **unincorporated Town of Hempstead** homeowners — a different person entirely. The memo names an RVC homeowner, ranks the sanitation takeover as the #1 pure-dollar lever, and never discloses that this lever's incidence excludes that exact homeowner. The strategist flagged that #1 "does ~nothing for racism" but buried that it also does ~nothing for *this bill*. That is the single strongest objection to the lead ranking: **its top dollar lever is incidence-mismatched to the subject it was written for.**

---

## Why the headline savings are overstated

### 1. The county-guarantee firewall blocks assessment reform from reaching 60-65% of the bill
The prompt's structural point is decisive and the report concedes it (Sec. 5): under the 1938 charter, **school districts are held harmless from over-assessment** — the county eats the refund including the school share. Consequence the strategist never carries through: fixing assessment accuracy **cannot lower school levies**, which are 60-65% of the bill. It only eases county-general levy (~15-20% of the bill) and even that as a *volatile certiorari liability*, not a steady rate. Lever #2 is therefore: (a) diffuse, (b) confined to the smallest non-school slice, (c) lumpy not recurring, and (d) front-loaded with a protest-wave cost (2018 Curran reassessment → 200K+ protests). The strategist's own "Mostly DIFFUSE … not a homeowner line item" admission should have *demoted* it; instead it sits at #2.

### 2. Lever #3's credibility rests on a bait-and-switch number
The strategist cites the realized **CWSSI $130.5M first-year savings (2018)** as the "realized analogue" validating an $80-160M school-back-office estimate. But the report (Sec. 4b) states that $130.5M was **dominated by a single Long Beach wastewater consolidation** — a one-time capital/utility project, *not* school back-office, and one that fell out of state-match eligibility. Borrowing a wastewater number to underwrite a school-administration estimate is a category error. Strip it and Lever #3 is **pure memo arithmetic** ("[ESTIMATE — this memo's arithmetic]," the report's own tag), 1-2% of an ~$8B aggregate, against counter-evidence the report supplies: Michigan food-service privatization savings *fully offset* by fees; OSC finding districts "could not verify" guaranteed EPC savings. And the BOCES subsidy is regressive to the goal: wealthy LI districts sit at the **36% aid floor** and "under-use it," so the districts whose homeowners you most want to relieve capture the *least* subsidy.

### 3. Diseconomies, not economies, at Nassau scale
Lever #4 (merger / county-wide district) is sold with a real-equity payload but the *dollar* case is worse than "weak" — it is plausibly **negative**. D-Y net savings: 31.5% at 300 pupils, 14.4% at 1,500, **near-zero at ~3,700** (Nassau average). AASA: diseconomies appear in *very large* districts. A county-wide ~190K-pupil district is deep in diseconomy territory; the "45% lower NoVA per-pupil" figure bundles regional wages/pensions/programs and the report itself says treat it "as a ceiling, not a projection." Add **salary/benefit leveling-up to the highest contract** (NYSUT/NYSSBA flag wage harmonization as a first-order offset) and capital adjustment (~half of gross savings) and the realistic operating delta is 2-7% gross, **mostly consumed before it reaches a levy** — and the wealthy side's rate *rises* to absorb the poorer side. As a homeowner-dollar lever for RVC, #4 is a tax *increase*.

### 4. Even Lever #1's surviving magnitude is pre-leveling-up
For the homeowner it *does* reach (unincorporated Hempstead), the 3x gap is gross. The report flags "union wage-harmonization on absorption can claw back part of the 3x gap" and "realization (vs. estimate) is the open question." So the one audited, recurring, pocket-visible lever in the whole memo is real but (a) for the wrong homeowner and (b) partially clawed back on absorption.

## Re-ranking on REAL, RECURRING, HOMEOWNER-VISIBLE dollars (for the named RVC homeowner)

The brutal truth the memo dances around: **almost nothing on this list cuts an RVC homeowner's bill in a recurring, visible way**, because RVC already self-provides the services the dollar levers attack, and the county guarantee firewalls the school slice from assessment reform.

1. **BOCES back-office shared services (#3)** — *only if* measured with hard savings teeth and stripped of the wastewater number. It is the one lever that (a) touches the 60-65% school slice, (b) needs no referendum, (c) is recurring. But honestly scoped it is low-single-digit % and regressive-to-uptake for rich districts. Tentative #1 by *default*, not strength.
2. **Village/county double-assessment elimination (#7)** — tiny but genuinely RVC-applicable, recurring, board-vote only. Small honest dollars beat large diffuse ones.
3. **K-12 marginal shared services / transport / EPC (#9)** — small, recurring, RVC-reachable, but only with measurement discipline (OSC's central finding).
4. **County-guarantee reform (#2)** — demoted: diffuse, lumpy, school slice firewalled, protest-cost front-loaded.
5. **Sanitation takeover (#1)** — demoted *for this homeowner* to near-zero on incidence grounds (RVC self-provides); remains #1 only for a Hempstead-unincorporated homeowner.
6. **Commercial tax-base pooling (#5)** — redistributive, not a saving; for RVC (commercial-adjacent wealthy side) a net *cost*. Honest equity lever, not a dollar lever.
7. **Cross-line merger (#4)** — dollar-negative for RVC; diseconomies + leveling-up + rate-up to absorb. Equity lever only.
8. **Village dissolution (#8)** — value-destroying for RVC (loses municipal electric); correctly withheld.

**Net verdict:** the strategist's pure-dollar topPick (#1) is the right answer to the wrong question — it optimizes a Hempstead homeowner's bill, not the named RVC homeowner's. For RVC specifically, the recurring-dollar opportunity is **modest BOCES/back-office trims + double-assessment cleanup**, both small, and the honest headline a skeptic must deliver is: *the structural savings that would actually move an RVC bill (the school slice) are firewalled by the county guarantee and flattened by Nassau-scale diseconomies — the big numbers in this memo are either one-time, diffuse to the county, or land on someone else's parcel.*
