The working hypothesis of this project since March 2026: accelerating voluntary senior-to-family housing turnover is the least-painful lever available to sustain RVC's school infrastructure — cheaper than overrides, gentler than cuts, and no one gets taxed out.

02What the fiscal mechanics actually are

Three mechanics are commonly gotten wrong — including in this project's own early drafts, corrected in the June 2026 validation:

  • Exemptions shift, they don't drain. Districts levy a fixed dollar amount; every exemption moves burden onto non-exempt taxpayers rather than reducing district revenue (Comptroller's exemption brief). There is no “$6.9M revenue gap” from senior exemptions — early versions of this analysis said so, and that framing was wrong.
  • STAR is Albany's money, not the district's (RPTL §1306-a). Each Enhanced→Basic STAR transition saves New York State ≈$2,058/yr ($3,147.01 → $1,089, 2025 final amounts, RVC Class 1).
  • Turnover's district upside is indirect but real: renovations grow the levy through the tax-base growth factor; enrollment-linked aid follows students; each ≈$820K sale (ACS median) pays ≈$3,280 in state transfer tax.

Why seniors stay put: the federal capital-gains exclusion ($250K/$500K), unindexed since 1997 — ~1.9M senior-owned homes now exceed it (AEI); benefit-timing friction in a move year; and misinformation — Enhanced STAR follows an eligible owner anywhere in NY, and as of 2026 the state auto-upgrades seniors at 65 and counts only resident-owners' income (income limit now $110,750). Age-based exemptions measurably increase staying put (NBER w25468); higher carrying costs shift large homes to younger families (Minneapolis Fed).

03A decision already on your calendar: §467

Two recent state changes land directly on RVC's boards:

  • The 65% tier exists now. Ch. 581 of 2025 (signed Dec 5, 2025) lets each taxing jurisdiction raise the senior-exemption maximum from 50% to 65% for the lowest-income tier (DTF tier tables; Nassau brochure).
  • The income ceiling may rise to $75,000 on July 1, 2027. The statute text of RPTL §467(3)(a) raises the maximum limit localities may adopt from $50,000 to $75,000 on that date — a local opt-in, ~11 months out.

The data gap

Both changes are humane in intent. Both also deepen lock-in and shift burden onto every non-exempt homeowner — in a village whose tax rate just rose 7.2%. The data to price these decisions is not public: no one outside the Assessment Department can say how many RVC parcels receive §467 by tier, or what an opt-in would shift onto other taxpayers.

04The asks

Village of Rockville Centre — trustees

  • Before any §467 expansion (65% tier now; $75K ceiling in 2027): publish a parcel-level fiscal note — parcels by tier, dollars shifted, per-household impact on non-exempt taxpayers.
  • Pair any expansion with mobility measures: a downsizing information program (the 2026 STAR changes are under-communicated good news) and zoning/ADU steps that create somewhere to downsize to.

Nassau County — legislators

  • Direct the Department of Assessment to publish §467 and Enhanced STAR parcel counts by school district and tier — the transparency every downstream fiscal note depends on. (A FOIL request for RVC's counts is pending.)
  • Apply the same fiscal-note discipline county-side before adopting the $75K ceiling.

State — Sen. Canzoneri-Fitzpatrick (SD-9) · Asm. Griffin (AD-21)

  • Advance S3309/A5288 — Enhanced STAR continuity for seniors who buy after the levy date. S3309 cleared Senate Aging 7-0 on April 21, 2026 but stalled in Finance when the 2026 session adjourned; companion A5288 has not moved out of Assembly Real Property Taxation. A low-cost fix that removes a real move-year penalty.
  • Commission a fiscal note on a NY senior property-tax deferral enabling act (OR/MA/WA/TX models): relief without lock-in, repaid at sale.

Federal — Rep. Laura Gillen (NY-4)

  • Cosponsor H.R. 1340, the More Homes on the Market Act — doubles the §121 exclusion and indexes it. 146 cosponsors including LI-area members of both parties (Suozzi, Malliotakis); the bipartisan Senate companion S. 3332 is endorsed by NAR and AARP.

05What this brief does not claim

No aggregate “savings recaptured” or “exemption gap” dollar figure appears here, because the parcel-level data to compute one honestly is not yet public (§04). Assessment context: Nassau's roll has been carried forward since the 2020-21 reassessment; the 2026/27 tentative roll published Jan 2, 2026 at a Class 1 level of assessment of 0.1% (county FAQ) — a county-wide issue noted for completeness, not a village decision.
