# Clean-Slate Redraw of Nassau County Government Boundaries: Inputs & Risk Framework

**Quantitative public-finance modeling memo · Prepared June 10, 2026**
**Scope:** A "start fresh" redraw of Nassau County, NY — primarily its **56 school districts** (~$8B aggregate K-12 spend, ~190K students, avg ~3,700 pupils/district), secondarily its ~240 special districts. Question: what would a redraw plausibly save, by what mechanism, and at what risk?

**Labeling:** **[VERIFIED]** = primary doc or 2+ independent sources. **[REPORTED]** = single credible secondary source. **[ESTIMATE]** = a study's projection or this memo's arithmetic, flagged as such.

**Household divisor used throughout:** ~430,000 Nassau households (back-solved from OSC's $400M special-district levy = $946/household, 2004 → ~423K; rounded up for 2026). All $/household figures are **[ESTIMATE]** and rounded.

---

## HEADLINE

A **county-wide redraw is past most of the scale economies the literature can document** — Nassau's districts already average ~3,700 pupils, inside or above the empirical optimal-size band (~2,000–4,000). The defensible recurring prize is therefore **administrative/back-office, not pedagogical scale: ~2–6% of operating cost ≈ $160M–$480M/yr ≈ $370–$1,100/household/yr [ESTIMATE]** — and roughly **one-third to one-half of that is eaten** by union salary leveling-up, lost BOCES shared-services aid, and capital/diseconomy adjustment. The single most efficient move on the curve is **(c) mandated back-office/BOCES "virtual consolidation,"** which captures most of the addressable savings at a fraction of the political and transition cost of a full merger.

## PART 1 — THE COST-CURVE PARAMETERS (Duncombe & Yinger and successors)

### 1.1 The headline cost-curve numbers

**Duncombe & Yinger (2007), "Does School District Consolidation Cut Costs?", *Education Finance & Policy* 2(4):341–375** — panel of NY rural district mergers, 1985–1997. This is the load-bearing source. [VERIFIED]

| Metric | 300-pupil district | 900-pupil | 1,500-pupil |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Gross** operating $/pupil cut from **doubling enrollment** | **−61.7%** | (interpolates) | **−49.6%** |
| **Net** cut after adjustment (esp. capital) costs | **−31.5%** | — | **−14.4%** |
| Savings from **merging two like-sized districts** | **−22%** ("over 20%") | **−7% to −9%** | **~0% ("little, if any")** |

Source: [Duncombe & Yinger 2007, Syracuse SURFACE](https://surface.syr.edu/cpr/122/); [MIT Press](https://direct.mit.edu/edfp/article-abstract/2/4/341/10058/Does-School-District-Consolidation-Cut-Costs); [ERIC EJ896695](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ896695). Two key cautions the authors themselves raise: (1) **adjustment costs — overwhelmingly capital — consume roughly half of gross savings**; (2) states "should avoid subsidizing unwarranted capital projects." [VERIFIED]

**The shape of the curve, in words:** Per-pupil operating cost falls **steeply** below ~1,000 pupils, **flattens hard between 1,500 and ~2,000**, and the marginal scale saving from further doubling is essentially **zero by 1,500 and can turn negative** in very large districts.

### 1.2 Where economies exhaust + the optimal-size band

**Andrews, Duncombe & Yinger (2002), "Revisiting Economies of Size in American Education: Are We Any Closer to a Consensus?", *Economics of Education Review* 21(3)** — the canonical literature review. [VERIFIED]
- **"Sizeable potential cost savings may exist by moving from a very small district (500 or fewer pupils) to a district with approximately 2,000–4,000 pupils."**
- **Diseconomies of scale "may not set in until enrollments reach 6,000 students"** (lower in sparsely populated states due to transport costs). Successor work (Indiana, flexible-form studies) puts **minimum efficient scale ≈ 2,000** and the **cost-minimizing optimum ≈ 6,000–6,500 students**, beyond which **diseconomies dominate**.
- Source: [ERIC EJ647047](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ647047); [Experts@Syracuse](https://experts.syr.edu/en/publications/revisiting-economies-of-size-in-american-education-are-we-any-clo); review summarized by [Vermont Agency of Education research brief, 2015](https://legislature.vermont.gov/Documents/2016/WorkGroups/Senate%20Education/Reports%20and%20Resources/W~Agency%20of%20Education~Research%20Brief%20on%20Optimal%20School%20District%20Sizes~3-10-2015.pdf).

**Optimal district-size band (synthesis of literature): ~2,000–4,000 pupils for the bulk of scale gains; cost-minimizing point ~6,000–6,500; diseconomies beyond.**

### 1.3 Where Nassau sits on the curve — the crux

| Fact | Value | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Nassau avg district enrollment | **~3,700 pupils** (LI Index: 127 LI districts avg **3,735**) | **Inside the 2,000–4,000 optimal band.** Already past the steep part of the curve. |
| Nassau total K-12 enrollment | **~190,000** | A county-wide district would be **~30× the 6,000–6,500 cost-minimizing optimum** → squarely in **diseconomy** territory on the curve. |
| Duncombe-Miner-Ruggiero (1994), NY | — | **"Relatively few New York districts are strong candidates for full consolidation, although some may benefit from sharing of administrative and support functions."** [VERIFIED] |

**Bottom line for Part 1:** The Duncombe-Yinger machinery says Nassau's *average* district is **already roughly optimally sized**. A full county-wide merger does **not** ride the steep scale curve down — it overshoots the optimum into the **diseconomy** region. The realizable savings from a redraw are therefore **not classic scale economies** but **the elimination of duplicated fixed overhead** (56 of everything), which the cost-function literature treats as a separate, smaller bucket. ([LI Index figures: Rauch archive](https://www.rauchfoundation.org/news-resources/archive/long-island-must-shed-small-town-mind-set/); [Duncombe et al. 1994, ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/027277579400011T).)

## PART 2 — WHAT A REDRAW CONSOLIDATES + THE SAVINGS MATH

Base = **~$8B Nassau aggregate K-12 spend**, ~430K households. All ranges **[ESTIMATE]**; per-category % is of the $8B operating base.

### 2.1 Consolidatable overhead, itemized

| Category | Mechanism | Defensible recurring saving (% of $8B) | ≈ $/household/yr |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Superintendents & central admin** (56→1 or 3) | Eliminate ~50–53 superintendent offices. LI supt avg **>$243K**; 56 supts ≈ **$13–14M** in supt comp alone, plus assistant-supt/business-office multiples (typically 3–6× the supt line). | **0.6–1.5%** ($48–120M) | **$110–280** |
| **HR / payroll / finance / IT / purchasing** | Collapse 56 back offices to shared service centers; joint procurement leverages volume. | **0.5–1.2%** ($40–96M) | **$95–225** |
| **Transportation routing across lines** | Route buses to nearest school regardless of district line; eliminate deadhead/overlap. Contractors already carry >63% of NY riders. | **0.3–0.8%** ($24–64M) | **$55–150** |
| **Special-ed & out-of-district placement pooling** | Pool low-incidence/high-cost placements; in-source programs currently bought singly at premium. | **0.2–0.6%** ($16–48M) | **$37–110** |
| **Health-insurance buying power** | One ~190K-life pool vs. 56 small pools; self-insurance becomes viable. | **0.3–0.7%** ($24–56M) | **$55–130** |
| **TOTAL addressable (gross, pre-offset)** | | **~1.9–4.8%** ($152–384M) | **~$350–895** |

Pushing the upper category bounds together (aggressive but defensible) reaches **~6% / ~$480M / ~$1,100/household**; the conservative floor is **~2% / ~$160M / ~$370/household**. This is **consistent with the GOVERNANCE_REVIEW memo's independently derived "2–7% of operating cost"** administration-only range and its "1–2% of ~$8B = $80–160M" back-office figure. Supt-comp anchor: [Patch LI supt salaries](https://patch.com/new-york/massapequa/long-island-school-superintendent-salaries-ranked); [Empire Center](https://www.empirecenter.org/publications/57-li-school-officials-among-nys-highest-paid-public-educators/).

### 2.2 ONE-TIME transition costs (distinct from recurring savings)

| Transition cost | Magnitude (qualitative / where quantified) |
|---|---|
| **System integration** (SIS, payroll, ERP, network harmonization across 56 systems) | High; multi-year. No clean public figure for a merger this size — budget tens of $M. |
| **Contract harmonization & legal** | Renegotiate ~56× sets of CBAs + vendor contracts; litigation risk. |
| **Severance / early-retirement** for displaced admin | Front-loaded; partially offsets year-1 admin savings. |
| **Salary leveling-up (one-time step-up that becomes permanent)** | See Part 3 — the big one. Vermont priced **one** theoretical district's leveling at **>$5.5M** + **~$1M/district** added pension in years 1–2. [REPORTED] ([tribdem/PA & VT analyses](https://www.tribdem.com/pennsylvania/merging-schools-research-suggests-sweeping-statewide-consolidations-would-have-mixed-financial-results-for-pennsylvania-communities/article_acc022b0-a0c1-11e8-9df2-1f2e604e9341.html)) |

**Rule of thumb from the literature:** recurring savings are **real but lag** by 3–7 years; transition costs are **front-loaded**, so net cash flow is **negative in years 1–3** and turns positive only after harmonization settles. NY offsets this with **Reorganization Incentive Operating Aid (40% Foundation-Aid bonus, yrs 1–5, phasing out by yr 15) + ~30% building-aid bump** — but that aid rides on *Foundation Aid*, which is **structurally tiny for wealthy Nassau districts**, so the state carrot is weakest exactly here. ([NYSED Primer 2024](https://www.nysed.gov/sites/default/files/programs/regionalization/school-district-reorganization-a-primer_7.18.2024.pdf).)

## PART 3 — THE OFFSETS THAT EAT THE SAVINGS

| Offset | Magnitude / evidence | Effect on the Part-2 prize |
|---|---|---|
| **Union salary & benefit "leveling-up"** | On merger, salary schedules and benefits harmonize **to the most generous participating contract** (rarely down). Unionization/CBA itself lifts pay **~4–12%**; merger leveling concentrates that on the below-top districts. Vermont: **>$5.5M** to level one district; **~$1M/district** extra pension yrs 1–2. NYSUT and NYSSBA both flag wage harmonization as the **first-order** offset. | **Largest single eraser.** Plausibly consumes **30–60%** of gross admin savings, and it is **permanent**, not one-time. [REPORTED] ([NYSUT Fact Sheet 25-3](https://www.nysut.org/resources/all-listing/research/fact-sheets/fact-sheet-school-district-mergers-consolidations); [NYSSBA brief](https://www.nyssba.org/clientuploads/nyssba_pdf/GR-SIC-School-District-Consolidation-Issue-Brief.pdf)) |
| **Capital / facility adjustment** | D&Y: adjustment costs (mostly **capital**) cut **gross savings ~in half**; merged districts build/upgrade to equalize facilities. | Converts 1.9–4.8% gross toward the **lower** bound. [VERIFIED] |
| **Diseconomies of bureaucratic scale** | A ~190K-student district is **~30× the 6,000–6,500 cost optimum**; literature says diseconomies dominate past ~6,000. Bureaucratic layers, grievance load, slower procurement. | Caps and can **reverse** savings at county scale; favors 3-district or virtual options. [VERIFIED] |
| **Loss of BOCES shared-services aid** | Nassau's 56 districts **already capture some scale via BOCES** (36–90% aid ratio; 2025 budget doubled aidable salary cap **$30K→$60K**). A single county district is **too large to be a BOCES component district** → forfeits this state subsidy on shared functions. | **The savings you'd "create" by merging are partly already being banked** via BOCES — and merging can *lose* the aid that funds them. [VERIFIED] ([NYSSBA BOCES explainer](https://www.nyssba.org/news/2025/06/06/on-board-online-june-9-2025/what-every-local-school-board-member-should-know-about-the-boces-budget/)) |
| **County-guarantee firewall** | Nassau (one of two county assessing units) **refunds over-assessments including school/town shares** — school districts are **held harmless**, FY2024 county certiorari/claims **~$89.2M**. Consolidation **does not touch** this; school districts' indifference to assessment accuracy **persists** post-merger. | **Orthogonal** — a redraw of *school* lines leaves the biggest structural Nassau cost driver untouched. [VERIFIED] ([NIFA staff report Aug 2025](https://nifa.ny.gov/system/files/documents/2025/08/nifa-staff-report-8-7-25.pdf)) |

**Net of offsets:** the **~2–6% gross** prize realistically nets to **~1–3% of operating cost (~$80–240M/yr ≈ $185–560/household) once leveling-up, capital adjustment, and lost BOCES aid are subtracted** — and at full county scale the diseconomy term can push the *net* toward the bottom of (or below) that range. [ESTIMATE]

## PART 4 — THE RISK REGISTER

| Risk | What the evidence says |
|---|---|
| **Loss of local control / responsiveness** | LI Index 2009 polling: Long Islanders **value small-district responsiveness**; school-board access in county-run northern Virginia was **equal** (49% both regions), so the *access* fear is partly overstated — but the **2024–25 NYSED "regionalization" revolt** (a planning rule, not even a merger) shows the politics are radioactive: branded a "takeover," Hochul made it optional, legislators filed an "Our Schools, Our Rules Act." [VERIFIED] ([NBC NY](https://www.nbcnewyork.com/new-york/regionalization-plan-schools-long-island/6008542/)) |
| **Home-value capitalization (school-quality premium)** | **Black (1999, QJE)**: parents pay **2.5% more for a 5% test-score increase**; **~2.2 pp** per school-level SD of scores, identified across attendance-boundary discontinuities. **Successor — Bayer, Ferreira & McMillan (2007, NBER 13236)**: after correcting for neighbor-sorting, willingness-to-pay is **<1% for a 5% score increase** — i.e., the **pure school-quality premium is real but at the *low* end** once neighborhood demographics are stripped out. **Implication:** merging a top district into an average one **does compress the test-score signal** that homes capitalize, so a measurable (but **single-digit-percent, not catastrophic**) hit to top-district home values is plausible; the bulk of the "good schools" premium is entangled with neighborhood sorting that a boundary redraw does **not** erase. This asymmetric risk — top-district owners lose, average-district owners may gain — is the **engine of merger opposition**. [VERIFIED] ([Black 1999 QJE](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/114/2/577/1844232) / [PDF](https://matthewturner.org/ec1410/readings/Black_QJE_1999.pdf); [Bayer-Ferreira-McMillan 2007](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w13236/w13236.pdf)) |
| **Equity double-edge / secession blowback** | Merging can **desegregate and equalize tax base** (ERASE Racism: LI's fragmented districts institutionalize segregation; intensely-segregated districts average **~$10K less revenue/pupil**) — but the **Memphis–Shelby County** case is the warning: 2013 city-county merger → **2014 "de-merger,"** six affluent suburbs (Arlington, Bartlett, Collierville, Germantown, Lakeland, Millington) **seceded** to form municipal districts; remaining district **slashed budget, closed schools, laid off hundreds**. **Secession is the predictable elite response to a forced merger.** [VERIFIED] ([Chalkbeat](https://www.chalkbeat.org/tennessee/2017/6/21/21102787/memphis-shelby-county-spotlighted-in-national-report-on-school-district-secession/); [ERASE Racism](https://www.eraseracismny.org/home/1-resource/360-heading-in-the-wrong-direction-growing-school-segregation-on-long-island)) |
| **Legal / constitutional process (NY)** | Districts reorganize under **Education Law (§§314 master plan, 1801+ annexation/centralization/consolidation)**. **Centralization** is the most common form; only Centralization and UFSD/Common **Consolidation** create a *new* district. Requires **voter approval by referendum** (majority of qualified voters in each affected piece, or 2/3 of each board), run by boards under **District Superintendent + NYSED** guidance. The **Commissioner of Education can study/recommend** and historically order small mergers, but a **defeated proposal cannot be resubmitted for 2 years**. There is **no clean statutory path to impose a single county-wide district over 56 unwilling electorates** — it would require either 56 separate winning referenda or new state legislation. [VERIFIED] ([NYSED Guide to Reorganization](https://www.p12.nysed.gov/mgtserv/sch_dist_org/GuideToReorganizationOfSchoolDistricts.htm); [NYSED Primer Aug 2025](https://www.p12.nysed.gov/mgtserv/sch_dist_org/School-District-Reorganization-A-Primer-Aug-2025.pdf)) |
| **Political feasibility** | **Every Long Island merger attempt on record has failed**: Tuckahoe–Southampton (defeated at polls 2013 *and* 2014; low-tax district refused to absorb a higher-tax one); Elwood's proposals to 5 neighbors (**none accepted**, 2010–11); the 2024–25 regionalization revolt. The binding constraint is that **the low-tax / high-property-value district always votes No** because it bears the leveling-up and loses the brand premium. **Feasibility of a full forced merger ≈ near zero without state preemption.** [VERIFIED] ([27east](https://www.27east.com/southampton-press/southampton-students-protest-failed-merger-vote-2-1439429/)) |

## PART 5 — THREE SCENARIOS TO MODEL

All figures **[ESTIMATE]**, % of ~$8B operating base, ÷ ~430K households. Savings shown **net of Part-3 offsets** unless noted.

| | **(a) County-wide single district** (Montgomery-MD style) | **(b) 3 town-based districts** (Hempstead / N. Hempstead / Oyster Bay) | **(c) Virtual consolidation** — keep 56 districts, mandate shared back-office via BOCES |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Structure** | 1 district, ~190K students | 3 districts, ~40K–90K students each | 56 districts intact; central HR/payroll/finance/IT/purchasing/SpEd/transport routed through BOCES |
| **MD benchmark** | Montgomery County MD: **155K students, 1 district, ~$17,876–21,349/pupil** — proof of concept that county-scale *operates*, but its per-pupil cost is **not** below LI on a like-for-like basis once wages/programs are netted. ([MCPS](https://apps.montgomerycountymd.gov/BASISOPERATING/Common/Department.aspx?ID=MCPS)) | — | Builds on existing **CWSSI** (Nassau's 2018 plan led the state at **$130.5M** first-year, though dominated by one wastewater project) and BOCES channel. |
| **Gross recurring saving** | 4–6% ($320–480M) on paper | 3–5% ($240–400M) | 1.5–3% ($120–240M) |
| **NET recurring saving (post-offset)** | **~1–3% ($80–240M) ≈ $185–560/hh** — *diseconomy + lost BOCES aid + max leveling-up bite the hardest here* | **~1.5–3.5% ($120–280M) ≈ $280–650/hh** — *stays nearer the 2K–4K×3 sweet spot; best raw $ net* | **~1.5–2.5% ($120–200M) ≈ $280–465/hh** — *thin gross, but almost no offsets; aid-subsidized* |
| **One-time transition cost** | **Very high** (tens–hundreds of $M; 56-system integration, full CBA harmonization, severance) | **High** | **Low–moderate** (no boundary change, no facility equalization, no mass severance) |
| **Time horizon to net-positive** | **7–12 yrs** (deep J-curve) | **5–9 yrs** | **2–4 yrs** |
| **Risk level** | **Very high** — secession blowback (Memphis), top-district home-value hit, local-control revolt, diseconomy | **High** — same forces, smaller surface | **Low** — no merger, preserves local boards & brand; preserves BOCES aid |
| **Legal path** | None short of **state legislation** (cannot win 56 referenda) | Requires winning referenda or legislation | **Achievable by mandate/incentive**; BOCES + CWSSI frameworks already exist; 2025 BOCES aid cap doubling already nudges this way |
| **Political feasibility** | **≈ 0** (every LI merger failed) | **Very low** | **Moderate–realistic** — the Lundine Commission's and LI Index's own preferred path |

**Recommended modeling priority:** **(c) → (b) → (a).** Scenario (c) captures **most of the addressable savings (~$120–200M/yr) with the fewest offsets, lowest transition cost, fastest payback, and a legal/political path that actually exists.** (a) is the headline-grabber but is **dominated** on a risk-adjusted, net-of-offset, feasibility-weighted basis — it overshoots the cost-curve optimum into diseconomy and triggers the full Memphis/secession + home-value + local-control risk stack with no statutory route.
