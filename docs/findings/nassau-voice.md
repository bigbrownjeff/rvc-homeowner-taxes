# Does Nassau's 56-District Fragmentation Dilute Education's Political Voice — and What Would a Single Unified Superintendent Win?

**Public-administration / education-politics research memo · June 10, 2026**

**Labeling:** **[VERIFIED]** = primary source or 2+ independent sources. **[REPORTED]** = single credible secondary source. **[ESTIMATE/JUDGMENT]** = a study's projection, an analytic inference, or this memo's arithmetic.

**Scope anchor.** Nassau County, NY public-school enrollment 2023-24 = **195,895 students** across **56 districts** ([NYSED Enrollment Data, Nassau County, 2024](https://data.nysed.gov/enrollment.php?year=2024&county=28); 56 districts per [Nassau BOCES directory](https://www.nassauboces.org/schooldistrictdirectory)) **[VERIFIED]**. A single Nassau district would be roughly the **6th-largest in the United States** — larger than Broward County's district-run enrollment (~188K), DC (~100K incl. charters; ~50K DCPS-run), Boston (~48K), and Atlanta (~50K); behind only NYC, LAUSD, Chicago, Miami-Dade, and Clark County (~305K) ([Broward 2025-26 benchmark](https://www.browardschools.com/bcps-departments/strategy-innovation/demographics-enrollment-planning/maps-and-data/data/student-enrollment-counts/2025-26-benchmark-day-enrollment-report); [Clark County SD, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clark_County_School_District); [Boston enrollment 2024-25, Boston Globe](https://www.bostonglobe.com/2024/07/26/metro/boston-school-enrollment-report/); [DC Policy Center, State of DC Schools 2024-25](https://www.dcpolicycenter.org/publications/stateofdcschools2024-25/); [Atlanta enrollment, Axios](https://www.axios.com/local/atlanta/2024/06/18/charted-atlanta-public-schools-enrollment-dips)) **[VERIFIED]**.

---

## BOTTOM LINE (read first)

1. **Theory:** For *voice/representation specifically* (as opposed to service efficiency), the **metropolitan-reform / consolidation side has the better of the empirical argument** — fragmentation depresses and skews participation and produces no single interlocutor (Kelleher & Lowery 2004; Lowery 2000). The public-choice/Tiebout case is strong on **efficiency and sorting**, weak on **collective political voice**. **[VERIFIED, with the caveat that "voice" findings are contested and context-dependent.]**

2. **Already-aggregated voice:** Nassau education **already has** institutional aggregators — Nassau BOCES, the Nassau County Council of School Superintendents, NYSCOSS, NYSSBA, and the LI Senate/Assembly delegation. The delegation is **demonstrably powerful** (it beat Hochul on "save harmless" in 2024 and killed NYSED regionalization). But it does **not** speak with one education voice: in 2024 those same Nassau superintendents lobbied *against* regionalization to **preserve fragmentation**, and the LI delegation's documented record is to protect the **wealthier, enrollment-declining** districts (via save-harmless) while LI's **high-need** districts got as little as **1% of GEA and <4% of owed Foundation Aid** in 2016 — *despite* the LI Senate Majority Leader and Education Chair both being from LI ([AQE 2017](https://www.aqeny.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/NYS-Perpetuates-educational-inequity-on-Long-Island-final-2.pdf)). **[VERIFIED]**

3. **Counterfactual gains from one super:** Real but bounded, and mostly *administrative/bargaining*, not *formula*. Strongest wins: a **single ~$1.5B+ insurance/health bargaining bloc**, one transportation/energy procurement counterparty, **one interlocutor** for the County Executive and for Albany (replacing 56 competing asks with one), and the ability to **set countywide policy** (magnets, enrollment, desegregation, tax-base equity) that 56 districts structurally cannot. Foundation-Aid-*formula* leverage gains are modest — aid is statutory and shares-driven, and Nassau's added clout would partly *duplicate* an LI delegation that already wins the regional fights. **[ESTIMATE/JUDGMENT]**

4. **THE CATCH (decisive):** A countywide superintendent represents the **county median student and the largest enrollments** — Hempstead, Uniondale, Freeport, Roosevelt, Westbury (high-need, majority-minority) plus the big middle — **not** Locust Valley (~1,794 students, ~$55-57K/pupil) or Rockville Centre. "What could the Locust Valley super win speaking for the whole county" is **the wrong question**: under unification they would no longer be speaking for Locust Valley. A wealthy small district would trade a **loud, fully-controlled local voice** for a **~0.9% minority share** of a countywide voice **aimed at a poorer median**. That is not amplification; it is **subordination** — and it is precisely why wealthy districts, and their LI legislators, fight consolidation. **[VERIFIED logic; enrollment shares VERIFIED/ESTIMATE.]**

**Net:** Fragmentation dilutes education's *generic, sector-wide* voice modestly (no single CEO, collective-action drag). A unified Nassau voice would gain real bargaining and policy-setting power — **but it would serve the county's median and high-need students, the opposite of what a Locust Valley or RVC is paying its local premium to control.** The dilution thesis and the catch are the same coin: aggregation only "amplifies" if your interest *is* the median's. For the wealthy minority, aggregation is dilution.

## 1. THE THEORY — does governmental fragmentation dilute a sector's political voice?

Two camps, both born from the same 1961 origin point (Ostrom, Tiebout & Warren).

### Metropolitan-reform / consolidation view (fragmentation = weak collective voice)
- **Premise:** Carving a metro/sector into many small jurisdictions is wasteful, uncoordinated, and leaves **no single interlocutor** to bargain with higher governments; collective action across many units fails. Consolidating into one large general-purpose unit yields coordination and a single accountable voice ([Goodman, *Political Fragmentation & Economic Growth*](https://www.cgoodman.com/files/papers/frag-grow.pdf); [Urban Governance Reform Index, *Public Organization Review* 2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11115-012-0196-5)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **The strongest voice-specific evidence — Kelleher & Lowery (2004), *Urban Affairs Review*, "Political Participation and Metropolitan Institutional Contexts"** (336 municipalities, 12 metros): metropolitan **fragmentation depresses and *skews* turnout**, and **"the results lend far greater support to those favoring the consolidation of urban political institutions than those supporting further fragmentation"**; **satisfaction variance is much higher in fragmented systems** ([Kelleher & Lowery 2004](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1078087404264219)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **Lowery (2000), "A Transactions Costs Model of Metropolitan Governance"** (*JPART*): a frontal challenge to public-choice "hegemony"; boundaries define interests and property rights and **structure the distribution of political transaction costs** — fragmentation raises the cost of redistributive/collective action even if it lowers the cost of allocative sorting ([Lowery 2000, JPART 10(1):49](https://academic.oup.com/jpart/article/10/1/49/991773)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **Collective-action mechanism:** Olsonian logic — fragmentation multiplies the number of actors who must coordinate; larger, looser coalitions invite **free-riding** and divergent asks ([OpenStax, *Collective Action & Interest Group Formation*](https://openstax.org/books/american-government-3e/pages/10-2-collective-action-and-interest-group-formation); [*Publius* 2019, fragmentation & coordination risk](https://academic.oup.com/publius/article/49/2/352/5058953)) **[VERIFIED]**.

### Public-choice / polycentric view (fragmentation = competition, responsiveness; NOT inherently weak)
- **Premise:** Tiebout (1956) — households "vote with their feet," sorting into jurisdictions matching their preferences, disciplining government like a market; Ostrom/Tiebout/Warren (1961) — apparently "chaotic" fragmented metros actually show **order, responsiveness, and scale economies** via competition, contracting, and mutual adjustment ([Tiebout model, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiebout_model); [McGinnis, *Polycentric Governance in Theory and Practice*](https://mcginnis.pages.iu.edu/polycentric%20governance%20theory%20and%20practice%20Feb%202016.pdf); [Ostrom Workshop, *Defining Polycentricity*](https://ostromworkshop.indiana.edu/courses-teaching/teaching-tools/polycentric-goverance/polycentricity.html)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **Voice rebuttal:** many small governments = **more access points / venues** and lower individual exit costs (Hirschman's exit *substituting for* voice); "more fragmented regions may be better suited to find avenues for collaborative activity" ([*Publius* 2019](https://academic.oup.com/publius/article/49/2/352/5058953)) **[VERIFIED]**. Polycentrists argue inter-jurisdictional competition **checks bureaucratic over-reach** ([Goodman](https://www.cgoodman.com/files/papers/frag-grow.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**.

### Which side wins for K-12 specifically?
- **On efficiency/sorting: public choice holds up** for the very smallest units but is *mixed at scale* — Nelson (1987): decentralization lowers spending for multipurpose but **not single-purpose districts** (school districts are single-purpose); Dowding & Mergoupis (2003): fragmentation **not** more efficient than centralization ([Lincoln Institute, *Efficiency and Equity of Tiebout in the U.S.*](https://www.lincolninst.edu/app/uploads/2024/04/2117_1439_LP2006-ch04-The-Efficiency-and-Equity-of-Tiebout-in-the-United-States_0.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **On voice and equity: the reform side has the better of it.** Tiebout sorting in K-12 **aggravates segregation by race and income**, because school-district boundaries become "highly salient racial boundaries" and sorting tracks willingness-to-pay ([Lincoln Institute](https://www.lincolninst.edu/app/uploads/2024/04/2117_1439_LP2006-ch04-The-Efficiency-and-Equity-of-Tiebout-in-the-United-States_0.pdf); [UConn, *Tiebout Sorting & Residential Segregation Through a Racialized Lens*](https://cpilj.law.uconn.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/2515/2018/10/8.1-An-Examination-of-Tiebout-Sorting-and-Residential-Segregation-Through-a-Racialized-Lens-by-Alexandra-M.-Greene.pdf)) — exactly the ERASE Racism finding in the local memo. Fragmentation's "voice" is really **exit purchased by the affluent**, which is the opposite of a collective sector voice.
- **JUDGMENT:** For K-12 *political voice at the state/federal level*, fragmentation is a net drag — it produces 56 competing micro-asks instead of one, and the "competition" benefit accrues to **individual mobile households**, not to **education-as-a-sector** bargaining in Albany. But the public-choice point survives in one crucial form for Q4: a small wealthy district's "voice" is its **total local control**, and that is a real, capitalized asset it will not trade. **[JUDGMENT, grounded in VERIFIED sources.]**

## 2. DOES NASSAU EDUCATION ALREADY HAVE AN AGGREGATED VOICE? — Yes, several. They do NOT speak as one.

### The existing aggregators
| Body | What it is | One voice? |
|---|---|---|
| **Nassau BOCES** | Shared-services co-op serving all 56 districts; back-office, special-ed, purchasing | **No** — a *service* cooperative, not a political principal. Wealthy Nassau districts buy *less* from it (sit near the 36% aid floor), so it can't speak *for* them ([Nassau BOCES](https://www.nassauboces.org/schooldistrictdirectory); BOCES aid 36–90% floor/ceiling per local memo) **[VERIFIED]** |
| **Nassau County Council of School Superintendents (NCCSS)** | Professional assn. of all 56 supers + BOCES; self-described "leadership voice… advocates… on local, state and federal level" ([nassausuperintendents.org](https://www.nassausuperintendents.org/); [mission](https://www.nassausuperintendents.org/mission)) **[VERIFIED]** | **Partly** — a coordinating body, but it is 56 employees of 56 boards with divergent fiscal interests; it advocates the *common denominator* (mandate relief, "local control"), not a single program |
| **NYSSBA + NYS Council of School Superintendents (NYSCOSS)** | Statewide boards' / supers' associations ([nyscoss.org](https://www.nyscoss.org/)) **[VERIFIED]** | **Statewide** voice — dilutes Nassau-specific asks into a 700-district average; upstate-vs-downstate tensions live *inside* these groups |
| **LI Senate/Assembly delegation** | Bipartisan regional bloc; historically the LI Senate Republicans held outsized leverage in a narrow Senate majority ([AQE 2017](https://www.aqeny.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/NYS-Perpetuates-educational-inequity-on-Long-Island-final-2.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]** | **This is the real aggregator** — and it is a *geographic/partisan* bloc, not an education-sector voice |

### Do they speak with one voice? Evidence says NO — and the fragmentation runs along wealth lines
- **The tell:** When NYSED proposed BOCES-facilitated **regionalization** (mere *planning*, not merger) in 2024-25, the **Nassau superintendents themselves**, with County Executive Blakeman and the LI Senate GOP, mobilized to **kill it** and **preserve fragmentation** — backing the "Our Schools, Our Rules Act" to *ban* state-mandated regionalization ([NY Senate GOP release, 2024](https://www.nysenate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2024/steven-d-rhoads/members-long-island-senate-republican-conference-local); [Mattera/Blakeman release](https://www.nysenate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2024/mario-r-mattera/senator-mattera-joins-nassau-county-executive-blakeman)) **[VERIFIED]**. The county's aggregated body used its voice to **protect the 56-district structure** — the strongest possible evidence that the divergent interests of 56 districts *prefer* fragmentation to a unified voice.
- **Divergence along wealth:** AQE (2017) documents that LI's ~10 high-need, ~93% Black/Latino districts were **shortchanged even with a powerful LI delegation**: in the 2016 budget these districts received only **1% ($1.02M) of the $117M GEA** delivered to the rest of LI, and **none got more than 4%** of the Foundation Aid they were owed — "**despite the fact that both the Majority Leader and the Senate Education Chair are from Long Island**" ([AQE 2017](https://www.aqeny.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/NYS-Perpetuates-educational-inequity-on-Long-Island-final-2.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**. The LI "voice" served the wealthy and average-need districts, not the high-need ones — i.e., the aggregator's *median/dominant* interest is the affluent districts.

### Punching above or below weight in Albany?
- **ABOVE weight on protecting the affluent / status quo (cuts AGAINST a simple dilution thesis):**
  - **2024 "save harmless" win.** Hochul's FY2025 budget proposed ending hold-harmless, which would have **cut ~$43M from LI** and hit enrollment-declining districts hardest (e.g., **Long Beach: −22% aid, −$4.3M**). The Senate and Assembly **rejected it**; a bipartisan **Upstate Caucus + LI delegation coalition claimed victory** in protecting school aid and blocking property-tax increases ([City & State, 2024](https://www.cityandstateny.com/policy/2024/03/how-hochuls-education-budget-proposal-created-chaos-one-long-island-school-district/395039/); [Spectrum, "legislature rejects hold harmless cut"](https://spectrumlocalnews.com/nys/central-ny/politics/2024/03/12/legislature-rejects-hold-harmless-cut); [Rhoads/Nassau senators rally](https://www.nysenate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2024/steven-d-rhoads/nassau-county-state-senators-assemblymembers-and); [LI Senate delegation budget highlights, News12](https://longisland.news12.com/long-islands-state-senate-delegation-touts-highlights-of-budget-40240716)) **[VERIFIED]**.
  - **Crucial nuance:** Save-harmless **benefits enrollment-*declining* districts** — disproportionately **wealthier/whiter** suburbs — and it was won by a **regional + upstate coalition**, NOT by any unified Nassau super. The existing fragmented structure *already* wins the fights that protect the affluent. That is the deepest cut against "a unified super would win more": on the highest-salience recent fight, **56 districts + their delegation already won**, and a single super would have been **redundant** to that win — or, if it represented the county median, might have **traded save-harmless away** for redistribution toward high-need enrollment.
  - **Regionalization repulsed (2024-25):** LI killed even a planning mandate ([NBC NY, Nov. 2024](https://www.nbcnewyork.com/new-york/regionalization-plan-schools-long-island/6008542/)) **[VERIFIED]**.
- **BELOW weight on equity/adequacy:**
  - LI is owed **~$879M in Foundation Aid** (half to its 10 high-need districts) and the **Regional Cost Index is two decades stale**; the **"shares agreement"** boxes LI at **~12.96%** of aid increases vs NYC's ~38.86% ([AQE / City & State, 2025](https://www.cityandstateny.com/opinion/2025/11/opinion-new-york-must-stop-shortchanging-schools-yesterdays-data/409693/); [LI Press, 2024](https://www.longislandpress.com/2024/10/07/foundation-aid-cuts/)) **[VERIFIED]**. The chronic "LI gets shorted downstate" complaint is real on *adequacy* — but the binding constraint is the **shares formula and NYC's size**, not the *number* of LI districts.
- **JUDGMENT:** Nassau education is **not** voiceless. Its aggregated bodies and delegation **win** the status-quo-protecting fights and **lose** the redistribution-toward-need fights — because the dominant interest inside the aggregation is the affluent/median district, and because the binding levers (shares, RCI) are statutory. Fragmentation's marginal dilution is real but **second-order** next to *whose* interest the existing voice already encodes.

## 3. THE COUNTERFACTUAL — what one Nassau super (195,895 students) could plausibly win that 56 cannot

Ranked by defensibility. Magnitudes are **[ESTIMATE]** unless noted.

**A. Single bargaining bloc — health insurance, transportation, energy (STRONGEST).**
- Health insurance is the largest non-salary cost line; 56 districts negotiate 56 contracts. One ~196K-student employer is a **monopsony counterparty** to carriers. Local memo already prices special-district *insurance pooling + joint purchasing* at **$23.8–35.7M/yr** county-wide (Nassau Comptroller, via Lundine 2008) — schools are a far larger pool. **Order-of-magnitude:** if a unified super trims even **1–2% of Nassau's ~$8B aggregate school spend** via consolidated insurance/transport/energy bargaining = **$80–160M/yr** (this memo's arithmetic, consistent with the local memo's BOCES framing) **[ESTIMATE]**.
- Transportation: contractors already carry >63% of NY riders; a single countywide RFP has materially more leverage than 56 ([local memo §4d]) **[VERIFIED as to mechanism]**.

**B. One interlocutor for the County Executive and for Albany (HIGH value, hard to price).**
- Today the County Executive (shared services, CWSSI) and Albany face **56 competing asks**. A single super is **one phone call** — the metropolitan-reform "single interlocutor" benefit made concrete. Nassau's CWSSI already posted the **state's highest first-year savings ($130.5M, 2018)** when one entity could act ([Rockefeller Institute](https://rockinst.org/issue-area/review-plans-submitted-state-county-wide-shared-services-initiative-2/)) **[VERIFIED as to CWSSI]**; a unified school principal extends that to K-12. **[JUDGMENT]**

**C. Countywide policy-setting that 56 structurally cannot do (HIGH value, redistributive).**
- **Magnets, inter-district enrollment, desegregation, tax-base equity.** Only a countywide authority can run **magnets/controlled choice** across today's boundaries or pool commercial tax base (the Minnesota model the LI Index found **73% popular**) ([Schott Foundation summary of LI Index 2009](https://schottfoundation.org/the-latest/long-island-index-education-study-finds-inefficiencies-and-inequalities-mismatch-in-funding-versus-student-needs/)) **[VERIFIED]**. This is the largest *latent* prize — and the one most threatening to wealthy districts (see §4).

**D. Foundation-Aid-formula influence (OVERSTATED — modest).**
- Aid is **statutory, pupil-weighted, and shares-bound**. A 196K-student district raises Nassau's *salience*, but (i) it would **duplicate** an LI delegation that **already wins** the regional fights (§2), and (ii) **larger districts do not systematically win more aid** — formulas generally give *extra* aid to the *smallest* districts, and Foundation Aid scales with pupils either way ([Education Commission of the States, rural funding](https://www.ecs.org/how-states-allocate-funding-for-rural-schools/); [NCES, *Disparities in District Spending*](https://nces.ed.gov/pubs95/web/95300.asp)) **[VERIFIED]**. **Marginal formula gain: low.**

**E. Tax-cap override leverage (MIXED).**
- A countywide budget faces **one** 60%-supermajority override vote instead of 56 ([OSC, tax cap](https://www.osc.ny.gov/local-government/property-tax-cap/what-real-property-tax-cap)) **[VERIFIED]** — simpler, but a single override mixing rich and poor sub-areas is **politically harder to pass** than a wealthy district's easy local override. **Net ambiguous.**

**F. Unfunded-mandate relief (special-ed, health) — voice gain real, but shared.**
- Mandate relief is the *common* LI ask and already the delegation's banner ([NYSenate, O'Mara mandate relief](https://www.nysenate.gov/newsroom/articles/2019/thomas-f-omara/senate-approves-legislation-long-supported-omara-make-property)) **[VERIFIED]**. A unified super adds a credible single voice, but the win is **statewide-coalition-dependent**, not Nassau-deliverable alone. **[JUDGMENT]**

**Counterfactual verdict:** The defensible gains are **bargaining-bloc savings ($80–160M/yr range) + single-interlocutor coordination + countywide redistributive policy levers**. The *formula/lobbying* gains are **smaller than intuition suggests** because the LI delegation already wins those, and aid is statutory. **[ESTIMATE/JUDGMENT.]**

## 4. THE CATCH — a countywide super serves the MEDIAN and the BIGGEST enrollments, NOT Locust Valley or RVC

This is the load-bearing section. The whole "what could one super win" framing **silently assumes the super would advocate the asking district's interests at larger scale.** It would not.

### Whom would the unified voice actually represent?
A single super answers to a countywide electorate/board and allocates by the **county median and largest enrollments**. In Nassau that median-and-mass is:
- **High-need, majority-minority, large/declining-resource districts**: Hempstead, Uniondale, Freeport, Roosevelt, Westbury — LI's high-need districts are **~93% Black/Latino, ~70% in poverty**, and average **nearly $10,000 less revenue per student** than predominantly white LI districts ([AQE 2017](https://www.aqeny.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/NYS-Perpetuates-educational-inequity-on-Long-Island-final-2.pdf); ERASE Racism via local memo) **[VERIFIED]** — **plus the broad middle** of mid-size suburban districts.

### The enrollment-share arithmetic (why a wealthy small district gets OUTVOTED, not amplified)
- **Locust Valley CSD: 1,794 students** = **~0.92%** of Nassau's 195,895 ([NYSED 2024](https://data.nysed.gov/enrollment.php?year=2024&county=28); enrollment via [LI Press, 2025](https://www.longislandpress.com/2025/03/31/locust-valley-25-26-budget/)) **[VERIFIED enrollments; share is this memo's arithmetic]**.
- In a unified district, Locust Valley's specific priorities command **~0.9% of the weighted voice** and are **outvoted by definition**. Today they command **100%** of their own board, budget, and Albany ask. The shift is **from majority-of-one to <1%-minority**.
- And Locust Valley's revealed preference is **high spending it fully controls**: **~$54,772/pupil (2025-26)**, rising to a planned **~$56,735/pupil (2026-27)** ([LI Press, 2025](https://www.longislandpress.com/2025/03/31/locust-valley-25-26-budget/); cf. CBC LI avg ~$41,934, state avg ~$36,293) **[VERIFIED/REPORTED]**. A countywide budget aimed at the median would **redistribute *away* from** that premium toward higher-need enrollment — the **opposite** of what the affluent district wants amplified.

### Is "what could the Locust Valley super win for the county" the wrong question? — YES.
- **Under unification, the Locust Valley super ceases to exist as Locust Valley's agent.** They become a sub-area administrator inside a 196K-student system whose objective function is the **county median**. Asking "what could they win for the whole county" **smuggles in a constituency swap**: the gains in §3 (redistributive policy, single bargaining bloc) are **wins for the median/high-need student**, often **financed by capping or pooling the wealthy district's local advantage**.
- **This is exactly why wealthy districts — and their LI legislators — resist consolidation.** They would trade a **loud, fully-controlled local voice** (capitalized into home values and total budget control) for a **diluted minority share of a countywide voice pointed elsewhere.** The 2024-25 regionalization revolt and the "Our Schools, Our Rules Act" are this preference in action ([NY Senate GOP](https://www.nysenate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2024/steven-d-rhoads/members-long-island-senate-republican-conference-local); [NBC NY](https://www.nbcnewyork.com/new-york/regionalization-plan-schools-long-island/6008542/)) **[VERIFIED]**. Tiebout/public-choice theory explains *why*: the affluent district's "voice" **is** its exclusive local control, an asset sorting created and capitalized ([Lincoln Institute](https://www.lincolninst.edu/app/uploads/2024/04/2117_1439_LP2006-ch04-The-Efficiency-and-Equity-of-Tiebout-in-the-United-States_0.pdf)) **[VERIFIED]**.

### The symmetry that resolves the whole question
- **For the median/high-need student:** fragmentation **dilutes** their voice (56 small asks, no countywide redistribution, no tax-base pooling) and a unified super would **amplify** it. The dilution thesis is **true for them**.
- **For the wealthy small district:** fragmentation is **maximal voice** (total local control) and a unified super would **dilute** it to <1%. The dilution thesis is **false for them** — it inverts.
- **Therefore the honest answer to "how much does fragmentation dilute education's voice" is: it depends entirely on *whose* voice.** There is no single "education's voice" in a county this stratified — that is the deepest reason the 56 districts have never unified. **[JUDGMENT, grounded in the VERIFIED record above.]**
